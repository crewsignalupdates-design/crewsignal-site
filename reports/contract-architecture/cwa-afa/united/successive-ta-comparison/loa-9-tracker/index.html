What LOA 9 does

Status: Foundational and load-bearing.

LOA 9 is the timing document for TA2. It does not merely restate that implementation takes time. It decides which items are effective on Date of Signing, which items have a stated later implementation date, and which items remain subject to later Joint Implementation Team work. That timing matters because the contract itself provides that, until a provision is implemented, Flight Attendants continue operating under the current 2016–2021 agreement unless the parties agree otherwise.

Why it matters: Much of the public debate around TA2 turns on text that exists in the agreement but is not immediately usable. This page keeps that distinction visible. A real gain can still be deferred. A strong summary claim can still overstate contractual effect if it ignores timing. And a DOS item is not the same thing as a JIT-governed item that still lacks a published implementation date.

Plain-English read: LOA 9 should be treated as part of the contract’s substance, not as back-office administration. On high-interest issues such as sit pay, vacation pay, Section 7.Q mechanics, reserve ordering, training deadhead, and hotel rules, implementation timing is part of the actual bargain.

How to read this tracker

DOS

The item is listed in Appendix A as effective on Date of Signing or on the first applicable DOS bid-period timing specified by LOA 9.

Fixed later date

The item is in Appendix B with a stated later bid period or month, such as January 2027 or January 2031.

JIT / TBD

The item is real contractual text, but its implementation schedule remains with the Joint Implementation Team rather than a fixed published date.

Editorial tags

Real, preserved, deferred, conditional, and derivative describe contractual effect, not messaging.

Current-baseline anchor: this timing architecture is not wholly new. The current 2016–2021 operating baseline already contains an implementation letter, a Joint Implementation Team, and large sets of DOS+, CMS, and ongoing items. What is different in TA2 is not the existence of implementation staging, but the specific placement of economically important items into DOS, fixed-later, and JIT buckets.

Implementation architecture: TA2 versus the current baseline

TA2’s implementation letter is not an anomaly. The current 2016–2021 operating baseline already used an implementation agreement with a Joint Implementation Team, DOS+, CMS-tied timing, and long lists of provisions that were not immediately self-executing. In that sense, LOA 9 is partly a continuation of the current operating model rather than a wholly new bargaining structure.

What changed is the placement of especially visible items. TA2 places wage rates, drafting pay, drug-test compensation, galley pay, International Purser pay increases, Language Qualified pay increases, short-crew pay increases, boarding pay, several per diem increases, selected Section 7 trip-trade items, and a large number of benefits and safety provisions into Appendix A as DOS items. At the same time, it pushes sit pay to January 2027, the vacation-pay increase to January 2031, numerous Section 7.Q tools to December 2026 through December 2027 or JIT, and major reserve mechanics into JIT-governed timing rather than fixed immediate dates.

Editorial conclusion: the existence of LOA 9 should not be described as a hidden defect unique to TA2. The more accurate criticism is narrower: TA2 places some of its most marketable gains outside the DOS bucket, which means readers must distinguish text, effective text, and later effective text.

Where summary materials can overstate contractual effect

The first overstatement risk is treating every TA2 improvement as if it were already live on DOS. That is wrong for sit pay, the 17-hour hotel item, several training changes, many Section 7.Q tools, many reserve mechanics, and the 3:30 vacation-pay value.

The second overstatement risk is treating preserved TA1 items as if they were newly won in TA2. Boarding pay is the clearest example. Against the current baseline, boarding pay remains a real gain. Against TA1, it is continuity, not a new TA2 achievement.

The third overstatement risk is flattening JIT items into certainty. A JIT-governed item is not imaginary, but its implementation timing is not yet the same thing as a DOS item or a fixed-date Appendix B item. The tracker should therefore keep JIT-governed items in a separate status category rather than blending them into immediate value.
