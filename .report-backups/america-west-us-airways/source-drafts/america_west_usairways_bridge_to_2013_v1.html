America West / US Airways Comparator

Bridge section: legacy East/West books to the 2013 combined US Airways/CWA-AFA agreement

The next comparison step is now clear. The key bridge document is the 2013 Flight Attendant Agreement between US Airways and the Association of Flight Attendants-CWA, archived by APFA as the LUS Red Book. APFA’s current contract page still lists the LUS Red Book under Previous Contracts & Contract Resources, alongside the Negotiations Protocol Agreement and the CLA (US Airways Bridge Agreement).

Working terminology: this 2013 document is best treated as the combined US Airways/CWA-AFA agreement, not yet the later American/APFA JCBA. It is the bridge between the pre-merger America West and US Airways legacy structures and the later American integration process.

Why the 2013 book matters

The 2013 agreement does two things at once. First, it consolidates the legacy East and West worlds into one US Airways agreement. Second, it shows how much of the merger architecture survived into the post-merger combined book. In Section 1, the agreement’s recognition clause expressly cites both the old US Airways flight attendant certification and the old America West certification, then recognizes AFA-CWA as the bargaining representative for the combined US Airways flight attendant group.

That matters because it gives us a clean comparison ladder:

  1. pre-merger America West/AFA legacy agreement;
  2. pre-merger US Airways East agreement family built on the May 1, 2000 book “as amended”;
  3. 2013 combined US Airways/CWA-AFA agreement (the LUS Red Book);
  4. American/APFA bridge and bargaining documents; and then
  5. the later joint collective bargaining agreement.

What the 2013 book preserved

At the level of structure, the 2013 agreement looks like a full modern contract book, not a thin merger memo. It runs through 39 sections and includes letters of agreement, including an implementation timeline letter. That alone makes it valuable for comparison work because it allows us to test whether the combined book preserved more of the West model, more of the East model, or a negotiated hybrid of both.

The early pages also show that the combined book continued to treat the operation in specifically merger-aware terms. Section 1 ties scope to US Airways flying and pilots, while also acknowledging the separate-pilot-operations period and the possibility that pilots of the merged carrier could ultimately operate with US Airways flight attendants. In other words, the 2013 book was not written as if all merger issues were already finished. It was written for a still-evolving integration environment.

Why this is the right bridge before the American/APFA JCBA

The representation-and-bargaining documents make clear that the 2013 US Airways/CWA-AFA agreement was not just a historical endpoint. It became one of the inputs to the next round of bargaining. In the AFA/APFA Agreement on Bargaining and Representation, the unions agreed that both the AFA/US Airways agreement and the APFA agreement as amended by the bridge agreement would be used to develop opening proposals for a single agreement. That means the 2013 book is part of the direct path to the later JCBA, not merely background.

Even more revealing, the 2013 US Airways agreement itself anticipated the American transaction. In a later letter of agreement, CWA-AFA agreed that it would jointly support a single-carrier application with APFA after merger completion. So the 2013 book is simultaneously a combined East/West agreement and a staging document for the next representation transition.

Immediate comparison questions

1. Recognition, scope, and successorship

How much of the pre-merger America West merger architecture survived into the 2013 combined book? The America West/AFA Section 1 language already contained scope, successorship, fence agreement, and merger handling rules. The 2013 book lets us test whether those concepts were carried forward, softened, or recast in a broader US Airways framework.

2. Pay philosophy

America West’s recovered compensation section shows a straightforward hourly longevity table with later step-up rates. The 2013 combined book gives us a much cleaner combined pay table for domestic and international flying. That will let us measure whether the combined agreement looks more like an uplift from the East restructuring-era system, a continuation of West assumptions, or a new hybrid.

3. Scheduling and reserve inheritance

The biggest unresolved issue from the East-side recovery is not whether a contract existed. It did. The problem is that East entered the merger through a bankruptcy-conditioned amendment and implementation process. The 2013 combined book is the first place we can test which scheduling, reserve, and line-construction concepts actually survived into the merged US Airways agreement.

4. Seniority and integration logic

On the West side, Section 19 plus the merger language in Section 1 and Side Letter #10 showed a stable seniority system sitting on top of merger-aware rules. The 2013 book should show what the parties were willing to codify after years of East/West coexistence and whether that system was robust enough to carry into the American merger without a complete rewrite.
