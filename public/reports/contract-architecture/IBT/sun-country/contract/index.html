Agreement Metadata

This analysis examines the March 2025 collective bargaining agreement text between Sun Country Airlines and the International Brotherhood of Teamsters. Because Article 31 still contains placeholder effective and duration dates, this report treats the uploaded document as the operative March 2025 agreement text rather than a fully dated execution copy. The agreement covers a carrier whose operation spans scheduled domestic flying, international flying, military charter, ad hoc flying, and other special operations.

Structurally, Sun Country's agreement is comparatively consolidated. The core work rules, pay architecture, reserve rules, international provisions, benefits, and grievance machinery are integrated into a single article sequence rather than scattered across a heavy legacy side-letter stack. As presented, it is easier to navigate than many legacy-network agreements.

The agreement is nevertheless built for variability. Articles 11 and 12 combine monthly bidding, reserve contact availability periods, open time, newly contracted trips, critical coverage, junior assignment, and time-available procedures into a system designed to absorb charter and disruption-driven volatility without abandoning seniority and pay protections.

This contract functions as a strong hybrid scheduled-charter reference within the CrewSignal Contract Architecture series. Its central design question is not whether the Company may move work at all, but under what procedural and economic conditions it may do so.

Contract Architecture Overview

Sun Country's Flight Attendant Agreement reflects a modern, operationally aware architecture. It is designed around electronic bidding, visible trip construction, reserve engineering, and explicit premium pay rather than inherited legacy patchwork.

Article 12 is the operational hub, but it works in tandem with Article 11 (Hours of Service) and Article 7 (Compensation). Scheduling protections are not isolated in a single veto provision; they emerge from the interaction of trip construction rules, footprint limits, reserve assignment order, minimum rest, monthly ceilings, and pay protection.

The agreement also reveals a distinctly charter-aware posture. It accommodates newly contracted trips, extraordinary flying, military and international duty extensions, and customer-driven report requirements, while still maintaining system seniority, scope protections, and structured premium pay when flexibility is used.

Implication Summary: Sun Country offers a comparatively clear and integrated framework with meaningful self-executing work rules. Its vulnerability is not legacy sprawl, but an architecture that still gives the Company real assignment flexibility through reserves, TAFA, and disruption management.

Scheduling & Assignment Framework

For lineholders, the agreement starts from real schedule ownership. Regular lines are built from trips and days off only, with a 72.5-99 hour range and a minimum of 13 days off at base. Known flying and deadheading must be built into trips for the monthly award, and newly contracted trips are separately identified and often bid by seniority rather than simply handed out.

That ownership is bounded, not absolute. When flying cancels or becomes illegal, the agreement routes the Flight Attendant into a structured TAFA/LWOP election system. Rescheduling is generally contained within a defined footprint, and when it occurs the Flight Attendant is protected by greater-of pay logic rather than an outright prohibition on reassignment.

The reserve system is highly engineered. Reserve lines contain at least 11 days off, four GDOs, built CAPs, and a limit on how much 24-hour reserve can exist systemwide. Assignments are sequenced by CAP, on/off day, accumulated credit, and seniority, which makes reserve use more rule-bound than purely discretionary.

Analytical Lens: The agreement still preserves aggressive back-end staffing tools. Critical Coverage creates incentivized volunteer flying; after reserves are exhausted, the Company may junior assign in reverse seniority. Sun Country constrains assignment through transparent sequencing, day-off rules, and pay protection, but it does not attempt to eliminate management flexibility during short-notice coverage failures.

Economic Structure

Sun Country's economic architecture is one of the contract's strongest features. Trip pay is built on a robust greater-of framework that compares block or actual time, duty rig, minimum trip credit, minimum average duty period credit, and trip hours credit based on time away from base.

That structure is supported by a 72.5-hour monthly guarantee, a 75-hour first-year reserve guarantee, full deadhead and ferry pay, over-85 incentive pay, holiday pay, long-day pay, critical coverage premiums, junior assignment premiums, and loss-of-off-day pay. The agreement repeatedly converts operational variability into defined compensation rather than leaving it uncompensated.

This matters because the contract openly contemplates irregular and non-routine flying. Instead of pretending disruption can be drafted away, it prices flexibility. That is especially visible in the premium treatment for drafting, operational-delay overages on the return-to-base segment, and vacation intrusion or rescheduling outcomes.

Key Insight: Sun Country manages flexibility economically and algorithmically. The Company retains tools to cover flying, but the cost of using those tools is usually explicit, auditable, and tied to the underlying event.

Enforcement & Dispute Resolution Architecture

Enforcement follows a traditional Railway Labor Act grievance-and-board model, but with several unusually practical supports. The agreement requires a progressive discipline system, notice of allegations before questioning, and Union representation rights during disciplinary investigations.

Non-disciplinary grievances are supported by defined filing windows, monthly hearings, access to payroll, benefit, personnel, and scheduling records, and written Company decisions after the hearing. Scope grievances are even stronger: Article 1 sends them directly to expedited System Board arbitration.

Operational disputes benefit from an evidentiary layer that many contracts only imply. Scheduling calls must occur on recorded lines, and the Union can obtain relevant recordings and scheduling data for dispute resolution. That gives the agreement real documentary support in areas where notice timing and assignment sequencing matter.

Corrective, not punitive remedies: Even with solid access rights and expedited paths, the enforcement model is still mostly make-whole: reinstatement, pay correction, credit restoration, or arbitral interpretation. The contract does not create standalone punitive penalties for routine violations.

Structural Strengths, Weaknesses & Comparative Flags

Strengths: A comparatively integrated single-document structure, strong scope and successorship language, a highly developed pay-and-credit architecture, engineered reserve rules, and unusually useful evidentiary support for scheduling disputes.

Weaknesses: Meaningful Company flexibility persists through TAFA, reserve assignment, operational-delay exceptions, critical coverage, and drafting; some protections depend on timely elections or positive contact; and the March 2025 text still carries transitional features such as PBS implementation bargaining and TBD Article 31 dates.

Comparative Flags: This is not a legacy-network, LOA-dependent architecture. It is cleaner, more modular, and more operationally explicit. At the same time, it is unmistakably a charter-aware agreement that accepts volatility and prices it, rather than trying to eliminate it at the front end.
