Contract Update & Comparison — 2025–2028 Agreement vs. 2022–2024 Baseline

Agreement: May 14, 2025 – May 14, 2028 Baseline: Nov 22, 2022 – Nov 22, 2024 Updated: February 22, 2026 Score (2025–2028): 20.6 / 30

Agreement Snapshot

  • Carrier: Norse Atlantic Airways AS (U.S.-based cabin crew under RLA jurisdiction)
  • Union: CWA-AFA
  • Operating frame: EASA-anchored legality/duty/rest constructs with an RLA enforcement stack
  • Architecture signal: Cross-border regulatory layering + long-haul rotation integrity

This update focuses on what changed in the 2025–2028 agreement compared to the 2022–2024 first agreement: where protections improved, where the structure stayed the same, and where drafting or tradeoffs create new risk.

Executive Summary

The 2025–2028 agreement is not a full architectural rewrite. The core “regulation-anchored” model remains intact (EASA definitions supply the legality spine; the contract supplies pay/credit rules, responsibility allocation, and an RLA dispute path). The biggest gains appear in compensation architecture (rates, per diem window, non-flying credit, and allowances), and in operational quality-of-life standards (hotel requirements, inflight rest facility expectations, base closure protections).

Top improvements

  • Per diem improved: higher hourly rate and paid from check-in to check-out (not just departure to arrival).
  • Non-flying credit improved: baseline three (3) block hours for most non-flying events, with additional credit for long standby/trainings/meetings.
  • QoL and stability: explicit hotel-room standards, upgraded inflight rest facility expectations, and base-closure protections with notice and benefit maintenance options.

Key continuity

  • Regulatory anchoring remains: EASA/Authority definitions still drive duty/rest/legality.
  • Scheduling spine remains stable: 12-day-off floor and published schedule protection framework remains central.
  • Enforcement remains conventional: grievance + System Board pathway still largely retrospective/time-delayed.

Primary risk / unfinished subsystem

  • Reserve protections remain incomplete: the agreement still defers bidding and reserve provisions to future “meet and negotiate,” and the pay article explicitly excludes reserve (as an example of non-duty) from pay/credit treatment.

Pay, Credit, and Economic Architecture

Hourly pay scale

The 2025–2028 agreement increases the pay scale across steps and continues annual increases through 2027. The monthly guarantee remains 60 block hours, and the agreement adds pay protection for flying lost through no action of the CCM.

Per diem (rate and duty-window coverage)

The per diem change is one of the most consequential improvements because it both increases the rate and expands the paid window to include pre-departure and post-arrival duty time (check-in through check-out).

Non-flying events

The new agreement moves toward a more unified “non-flying events” architecture. The baseline credit is higher, and long standby/trainings/meetings receive additional credit. The agreement also explicitly closes certain stacking paths by excluding pay/credit when a non-flying event shares a duty period with flying.

Allowances

The 2025–2028 agreement adds recurring allowances (uniform maintenance and communication) and updates alternate transportation support via a company match in the reimbursement program.

Legalities, Scheduling, and Operational Reliability

Roster transparency

A notable clarity improvement is the requirement that all hours of duty be accurately reflected on the roster, including pick-up time, deadhead time, check-in, and check-out time. This increases auditability and reduces “hidden time.”

Security-search check-in and security pay

The agreement introduces a longer check-in for flights requiring security searches, and adds a security-pay provision. Structurally, this is a tradeoff: additional time obligations become explicit, but compensation is also engineered into the pay system.

Inflight rest facilities

The agreement adds concrete expectations about rest facility equipment and cleaning cadence, which supports fatigue management in long-haul rotation operations.

Quality of Life and Reserve Protections

Sick bank improvements (with documentation triggers)

The sick bank expands to ten days and introduces donation and conversion mechanics. However, the agreement also introduces earlier documentation/notice requirements for extended illness.

Hotels

The hotel article is significantly more enforceable because it moves from general “clean/safe/quiet” expectations into explicit room standards (Wi‑Fi, quiet placement, non-smoking, and more).

Reserve: the biggest remaining gap

The agreement still does not fully engineer reserve as a complete subsystem. It continues to defer bidding and reserve language to future “meet and negotiate” work, and it explicitly treats reserve as an example of non-duty that receives no pay/credit. This is the clearest remaining high-risk zone in quality-of-life architecture, especially for a carrier where availability constructs can become the operational pressure valve during disruption.

What Stayed the Same vs. What Shifted

Stayed the same

  • Regulatory anchoring: duty/rest/legality definitions remain EASA/Authority-based.
  • Minimum days off and published schedule day-off protection framework.
  • RLA enforcement stack: grievance + System Board architecture.

Shifted materially

  • Per diem: higher rate and expanded paid window (check-in → check-out).
  • Non-flying credit: higher baseline value and more modular structure.
  • Hotel/rest/basing stability: more enforceable QoL provisions, including base closure language.

Potential ambiguity / drafting watch items

  • Guarantee deduction wording: compare the unpaid-status guarantee reduction language across agreements to ensure the intended meaning is preserved.
  • Seniority tie-break logic: the agreement introduces a random draw for new hires while the definitions section retains an “older is more senior” tie-break concept.
  • Reserve subsystem completeness: still not fully built into the contract’s scheduling and pay architecture.
