Contract Architecture Analysis

Effective Period: May 1, 2020 – April 30, 2024
Amendable since: May 1, 2024

Agreement Metadata

Horizon Air’s agreement reflects a regional-carrier bargaining environment with a conventional article-based structure and a comparatively systems-forward organization: core pay/credit and expenses appear early, followed by scheduling systems (lineholder and reserve), then benefits, seniority, and enforcement.

Primary document: Open the CBA PDF.

Contract Architecture Overview

The agreement is modular: it separates economic rules (credit/premiums), legality limits (hours of service/rest), and assignment systems (lineholder vs reserve) into distinct clusters. This reduces interpretive load relative to highly cross-referenced agreements and supports time-sensitive operational use.

Scheduling & Assignment Framework

Scheduling controls are concentrated in the lineholder scheduling article and a dedicated reserve article. Reserve operates as a full subsystem (categories/standby/assignment ordering), improving clarity but increasing the importance of procedural compliance.

Economic Structure

The pay/credit framework relies on defined credit drivers and premium constructs rather than broad discretionary policy language. This supports auditability, but edge cases can turn on “greater of” calculations and non-duplication logic.

Enforcement & Dispute Resolution Architecture

Enforcement follows the standard airline pattern: grievance steps culminating in arbitration/System Board mechanisms. Structural strength depends on whether remedies are timely and deterrent versus primarily corrective after delay.

Structural Strengths, Weaknesses & Comparative Flags

Strength: clear subsystem separation (pay, legality, lineholder scheduling, reserve) improves navigability and operational usability. Comparative flag: as with many regionals, practical force may depend on post-violation correction rather than self-executing constraints, especially during irregular operations.
