CWA-AFA · Horizon Air

Contract Architecture Analysis

Effective Period: May 1, 2020 – April 30, 2024

Agreement Metadata

This agreement reflects a regional-carrier bargaining environment with a conventional article-based structure and a comparatively “systems-forward” organization: core pay/credit and expenses appear early, with dedicated subsystems for scheduling (lineholder) and reserve, followed by benefits, seniority, and enforcement. It is designed to be operationally applied—supporting day-to-day decision-making during awards, legality questions, and irregular operations.

Primary document: Horizon Air CBA PDF.

Contract Architecture Overview

The Horizon Air agreement is structurally modular: it separates economic rules (credit and premium constructs), legality limits (hours of service/rest), and assignment systems (lineholder vs reserve) into distinct clusters. This improves navigability and reduces interpretive load relative to highly cross-referenced agreements.

Implication: The contract’s practical strength tends to come from subsystem clarity—outcomes are more predictable when triggers and classifications (lineholder vs reserve) are applied consistently by scheduling and payroll.

Scheduling & Assignment Framework

Scheduling and assignment rules are organized around defined lineholder processes and a dedicated reserve subsystem. Reserve is treated as a full operational control layer (categories/standby/assignment ordering), which supports coverage variability but requires careful compliance with procedural steps.

Analytical lens: Subsystem separation improves usability; enforcement reliability depends on clear triggers and consistent application during reschedules and irregular operations.

Economic Structure

The economic framework is organized around defined pay/credit drivers and premium constructs rather than broad discretionary “policy” language. That supports auditability, but edge cases can turn on “greater of” calculations and non-duplication rules where multiple credits could apply.

Key insight: Earnings reliability depends less on the presence of premiums and more on whether credit rules are applied consistently to actual operations.

Enforcement & Dispute Resolution Architecture

The enforcement model follows the standard airline pattern: defined grievance steps culminating in arbitration/System Board mechanisms. The structural question is not whether a process exists, but whether remedies are timely and deterrent versus primarily corrective after delay.

Structural asymmetry: As in many agreements, the burden of initiation and persistence often rests on the employee side, while management can benefit from procedural delay unless timelines carry meaningful consequences.

Structural Strengths, Weaknesses & Comparative Flags

Strengths: subsystem modularity (pay, legality, lineholder scheduling, reserve) improves navigability and supports operational use. Weaknesses: like many regionals, practical force can depend on post-violation correction rather than self-executing constraints, especially during irregular operations.
