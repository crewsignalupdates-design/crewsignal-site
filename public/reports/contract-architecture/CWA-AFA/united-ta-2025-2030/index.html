CWA-AFA · United Airlines

2025–2030 Tentative Agreement Analysis (Rejected)

Union: CWA-AFA · Status: Rejected tentative agreement · Proposed duration clause: 2025.07.30–2030.07.30

Editor’s Note: United flight attendants rejected this tentative agreement on July 29, 2025. Because it was not ratified, it did not become controlling contract language. CrewSignal analyzes it here as a publicly posted proposal for structural comparison only.

Status and analytical scope

This page analyzes a rejected proposal, not a controlling agreement. The posted tentative agreement included a duration clause that would have made the agreement effective on July 30, 2025 and continued it through July 30, 2030, but those dates never became operative because the membership voted the TA down.

That distinction matters for comparison work. This page can show what United bargaining publicly put on the table in 2025, but any statement about current operative rights should be anchored to the controlling United agreement, not this rejected TA. Readers looking for live contract architecture should use the main United contract analysis.

Pay scale and early-career economics

The clearest publicly verifiable gains in the rejected TA were economic. Section 4.A proposed 1st Year base rates of $36.92 at DOS, $38.03 at DOS + 1, $39.17 at DOS + 2, $40.54 at DOS + 3, and $42.06 at DOS + 4. It proposed 2nd Year rates of $39.15, $40.32, $41.53, $42.98, and $44.59, and 3rd Year rates of $41.65, $42.90, $44.19, $45.74, and $47.46. The table continued through 13th Year+, topping out at $84.78 at DOS and $96.58 at DOS + 4.

Section 4.B.1 also proposed incentive pay for all block hours flown in excess of 200:00 per calendar quarter, including vacation and deadhead, up to a maximum of 330:00, except that no incentive pay would apply for block hours flown in excess of 110:00 in a bid month. Section 4.C continued pay protection to 71:00 for any awarded line with less than 71:00 original flight time line projection.

Comparatively, this is the strongest documented feature of the public TA. The proposal would have materially increased rate-card value, especially in the early years, but it would have done so inside the same pay-and-credit architecture rather than through a structural redistribution of operational control.

Reserve quality of life

The rejected TA did not read like a clean reserve rewrite. The public text preserved a detailed reserve system built around RAP assignment, move-up line preferencing, sequencing of days off, and restoration rules.

RAP preference categories remained Early (0000 to 0559), Mid (0600 to 1159), Afternoon (1200 to 1759), Late (1800 to 2359), and Full (24:00 availability). In move-up line preferencing, a Reserve could specify up to four scheduled days off as protected days. The public text also continued restoration language when assignments reached into a block of days off, and it allowed the Company to build Reserve lines with up to sixteen (16) calendar days off in a month, subject to a proportionate reduction in guarantee.

For comparative purposes, the structural point is continuity. The TA kept reserve administration dense and preference-driven. It did not publicly present a simple, self-executing reserve redesign that would have sharply reduced day-to-day company assignment authority.

Insurance and healthcare

The public TA included a full Section 29 benefits article rather than a short placeholder section. The posted text continued medical, dental, vision, flexible spending account, retiree medical, life and accident, and long-term disability architecture.

One benefits formula that is safe to preserve exactly is the contribution rule for a Select Regional Medical Plan, the Core Medical PPO, or the Core Medical EPO: the monthly contribution equals the total monthly cost of the plan minus the amount of the Company contribution that would apply for the same coverage tier under the Traditional Medical PPO.

That is best understood as continuity with negotiated formulas and plan-specific cost-sharing, not as a wholesale healthcare redesign. If this page later adds plan-cost comparisons, those numbers should be quoted directly from Section 29 rather than summarized from campaign material.

Bidding, scheduling, and IRROPS protections

The bidding and assignment architecture also remained process-heavy. The public move-up line text continued preference fields such as latest arrival, equipment, position, protected days off, pairing number, International Purser, and language qualification, with a move-up line treated as a match only when all requested criteria were satisfied.

On operational disruption, the posted TA retained an irregular-operations section and continued explicit holding-time limitations. Section 6.N.3 limited holding time to four (4) hours at any one point or a total of five (5) hours during any on-duty period.

In comparative terms, that means the TA was not a short-form scheduling reset. It proposed added economic value and maintained a detailed procedural structure, but it did not publicly show a broad conversion to self-executing IRROPS bars or a major narrowing of company control over reassignment and coverage mechanics.

Retirement provisions

The retirement story in the public TA is also continuity-heavy. Section 29 preserved 401(k)-based administration, including in-service withdrawals at age 59½ or for financial hardship, participant loans, quarterly statements, and a Retirement Board for disputes.

The posted document also still carried legacy retirement material such as CARP, NPP, and the DB Plan Discussion LOA. That DB discussion language is a framework for further talks about whether developing a cost-neutral defined-benefit plan is practical; it is not a ratified restoration of a new defined-benefit pension.

For comparative work, retirement should therefore be framed as a continuity-and-governance issue rather than a pension-reset issue.

Comparative takeaway

As a rejected proposal, the 2025–2030 TA is most useful as a public marker of United bargaining direction in 2025. Its clearest documented gains were pay scale and early-career economics. The public text also preserved a dense reserve, bidding, scheduling, benefits, and retirement architecture rather than simplifying those systems into short, self-executing rules.

That makes this page valuable for comparison, but only if it stays disciplined about status. The TA can show what United proposed. It cannot be treated as operative contract language, and it should not be used as the live baseline for cross-carrier comparisons without separately anchoring those comparisons to the controlling United agreement.
