Section 19 — Safety, Health and Security

Current CBA p.179TA1 p.261TA2 p.237

Status: Strong non-economic improvement area.

Section 19 is one of the clearer practical positives. TA2’s implementation materials identify multiple date-of-signing gains around safety access and crisis response, including MEC Safety Chair access to irregular-operations reports, provision of the Inflight Services emergency manual, positive-space travel and additional union-representative access for foreign-country accidents, and continued monthly health/safety/security meetings.

Why it matters: This section improves how quickly and effectively representatives can act during serious operational events. For a safety-sensitive workgroup, that is more than symbolic.

Assessment: Improvement. One of the better non-economic arguments in favor of the package.

Cybersecurity policy and discipline risk

This report treats cybersecurity as a management-policy layer, not as a separate negotiated discipline article. The internal policy materials reviewed for this report describe a high-control compliance environment built around monitoring, device control, record retention, and anti-ephemeral-messaging expectations. Because those materials are non-public, this report generalizes the company-policy side and focuses specifically on the contractual impact.

The contractual point is narrower and more important: broader cyber policy may expand the range of company rules, system logs, device-related evidence, and records-retention expectations that appear in an investigation, but it does not create a separate discipline system outside the agreement. Any resulting discipline still runs through the same personnel-file, investigation, grievance, and arbitration machinery discussed in Sections 22, 23, and 24.

Reader takeaway: Cyber policy can widen the evidence base and the kinds of conduct that trigger investigation, but the due-process question remains contractual. That is why this issue belongs next to the safety/security section and then points directly forward into the discipline-and-enforcement sections.

Section 22 — Personnel Files

Current CBA p.193TA1 p.280TA2 p.253

Status: Meaningful improvement area, though still somewhat messy in draft architecture.

Section 22 becomes more favorable than the current baseline in several visible ways. Occupational injury or illness occurrences do not generate attendance points, non-disciplinary dependability or job-performance notations clear after one year instead of two, and the newer Performance Track reduces Performance Warning 2 to 12 months. The catch is that legacy and newer disciplinary language overlap, which can make the section look internally inconsistent if read too casually.

Why it matters: A contract does not just need rights; it needs workable personnel-file and progressive-discipline architecture. This section is better than the current contract, but it should be read carefully because the draft still contains overlapping old and new language.

Assessment: Meaningful improvement relative to the current CBA, but largely preserved from TA1 and still textually messy in places.

Section 23 — Investigations & Grievances

Current CBA p.196TA1 p.288TA2 p.260

Status: One of the stronger institutional improvements relative to the current baseline.

Section 23 shows a more developed investigation and dispute-resolution architecture than the current contract, including 48-hour notice before most discharge-track investigatory meetings, evidence disclosure, written notice, structured timelines, locally based dispute resolution, and quarterly settlement-conference cadence. TA2 appears mostly to preserve and operationalize that architecture rather than newly create it beyond TA1.

Why it matters: A contract’s value depends on whether Flight Attendants can realistically enforce it. More notice and stronger process improve the practical balance of power even when headline economics stay mixed.

Assessment: Improvement versus the current baseline; more carry-forward than fresh TA2 invention, but still one of the better sections in the package.

Section 24 — System Board of Adjustment

Current CBA p.209TA1 p.305TA2 p.276

Status: Clear process improvement.

The arbitrator panel expands from eleven to thirteen, and scheduled-hearing capacity improves. Relative to TA1, TA2 appears largely to preserve that gain rather than newly create it. Relative to the current contract, however, it is a concrete institutional upgrade.

Why it matters: System Board capacity affects throughput, scheduling flexibility, and the speed of final dispute resolution. For a large workgroup, that matters materially.

Assessment: Clear improvement relative to the current CBA; essentially carried forward from TA1 into TA2.
