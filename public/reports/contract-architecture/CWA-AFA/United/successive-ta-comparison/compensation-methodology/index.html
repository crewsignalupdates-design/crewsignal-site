Section 4 — Compensation

Current CBA p.31 TA1 p.38 TA2 p.35 TA2 Boarding Pay p.45 TA2 Sit Pay p.46

Status: Mixed and structurally important.

Section 4 is where TA2 most clearly tries to answer the rejection of TA1, but the answer remains incomplete. TA2 sweetens selected junior and some mid-scale hourly checkpoints, adds a later terminal year, and adds a genuine new pay item in sit pay. At the same time, many senior-scale readers will still see weaker later equivalent checkpoints than they expected, premium position economics remain largely unchanged, and the 3:30 vacation-pay value is still delayed until the January 2031 bid period.

Why it matters: Compensation cannot be reduced to a single headline rate table. TA2 still sits inside a fundamentally hourly pay structure, and that matters for how boarding, sit time, reserve, rigs, and trip shape are monetized. The section is stronger than the current baseline and in places stronger than TA1, but it is still not a clean all-scale compensation win.

Assessment: Real front-end gains, real new sit pay, but still a mixed compensation section because value remains uneven, premium structure is mostly preserved, and some important economics remain delayed.

Methodology — hourly versus TFP is not a direct comparison

United still pays from an hourly framework. TA2 adds boarding pay and sit pay, but both are layered on top of hourly rates rather than replacing the pay unit. Boarding pay is fifty percent of the Section 4.A rate, and sit pay is one minute paid for every two minutes of scheduled ground time over two hours and thirty minutes; both are pay only and not credit. That means TA2 adds real money, but it does not turn United into a TFP contract.

Alaska is the clearest formal conversion comparator because Alaska’s own CBA publishes “Block Hour Equivalents of Base TFP Step Rates” for industry comparison purposes only while expressly stating that Alaska Flight Attendants are not paid in block hours. Alaska’s LOA 16 then shows how Alaska itself normalizes hourly carriers against TFP carriers: it converts hourly scales using a twelve-month trailing ratio of scheduled “hard” TFP to scheduled block hours, caps annual movement in that ratio, uses a 2025 baseline conversion ratio of 1.11, and reduces carriers without boarding pay by 9.6% before averaging. Alaska’s own contract therefore confirms that cross-carrier pay comparisons require explicit assumptions and cannot be reduced to a single headline table.

Southwest is the clearest architecture comparator. Southwest defines TFP as Trips for Pay and then prices compensation through TFP-based minimums and reserve values rather than through raw block hours alone. Its current structure uses an Average Daily Guarantee of 6.5 TFP, a Duty Period Minimum of 4.0 TFP, a Duty Hour Ratio of .74 TFP for each duty hour, and a Trip Hour Ratio of 1.0 TFP for each 3:00 away from domicile. Southwest reserve pay is also expressed directly in TFP, with a monthly reserve guarantee ranging from 78 to 110.5 TFP at 6.5 TFP per reserve day.

What advantage does a carrier get from TFP? Without subjective speculation, the structural advantage is clear: a TFP system prices the pairing as a production unit rather than compensating only raw block. That allows management to embed duty-day minimums, time-away-from-base ratios, reserve-day values, boarding economics, holiday premiums, and other trip-shape assumptions inside one pay architecture. Whether that is better for workers depends on the surrounding rigs, reserve monetization, overrides, retirement design, and schedule protections — not on the existence of TFP by itself.

Key conclusion: TA2’s pay story is not “hourly versus TFP, and TFP automatically wins.” The stronger statement is narrower and more defensible: United remains an hourly contract with meaningful new add-pay items, while Alaska and Southwest show two different ways TFP systems can embed more of the workday into the pay architecture.
