United / CWA-AFA

Rejected 2025–2030 TA vs. Proposed 2026–2031 TA

First full report draft for CrewSignal

This report compares the rejected 2025–2030 tentative agreement with the proposed 2026–2031 tentative agreement section by section, then places the proposed agreement against the broader industry compensation benchmarks most frequently cited by United flight attendants: Southwest and Alaska. The goal is not to advocate for a yes or no vote, but to identify what materially changed, what did not, what may be overstated in summary materials, and what readers should understand before deciding how to vote.

How to read this report

This comparison separates four different questions that are often blurred together in ratification summaries:

  • What changed in the text?
  • What changed in actual economic value or day-to-day working life?
  • What is immediate and what is delayed?
  • What remains weaker than the rejected deal, even if the new TA improves other areas?

The most important methodological rule in this report is simple: the Letters of Agreement are part of the contract architecture, not optional appendices. A reader who does not parse the letters — especially the Implementation Agreement — will not fully understand what this TA actually delivers and when.

Executive summary

The proposed 2026–2031 TA is not a wholesale rebuild of the rejected 2025–2030 TA. It is better understood as a targeted revision. Some sections are meaningfully better. Some mostly carry forward the rejected TA’s structure. A few important items remain weaker than the rejected deal or are delayed so far into the agreement that their near-term value is materially reduced.

The strongest arguments for the proposed TA are found in:

  • selected base-rate improvements, especially at junior and some mid-scale checkpoints
  • real gains in paid leave
  • higher sick-bank caps
  • selected benefits / matching / retiree medical extension improvements
  • stronger safety-process access
  • better grievance / System Board infrastructure
  • partial reserve modernization through RAPs

The strongest arguments against it are:

  • many senior-scale pay checkpoints are slightly worse later than in the rejected TA
  • premium position pay was not meaningfully improved
  • 3:30 vacation pay was delayed until January 2031 instead of being immediate
  • important work-rule items are often phased in, sometimes as late as 2027–2031
  • reserve modernization exists, but still falls short of the stronger Southwest model
  • retiree medical sunset was extended, not eliminated
  • reassignment under Section 7.Q remains legally dense, operationally contentious, and imperfectly drafted

The most accurate one-sentence summary is: The proposed TA is neither the disaster some members may fear nor the comprehensive fix some summaries may imply. It is a mixed agreement with genuine gains, genuine weaknesses, and a large amount of value that depends on delayed implementation rather than immediate delivery.

Section-by-section first look

Section 1 — Recognition, Successorship and Mergers

Assessment: No material change

The core recognition, successorship, merger, seniority-integration, separate-operations, and expedited-arbitration framework appears materially unchanged. The second TA does not seem to use Section 1 to deliver a major merger or scope-adjacent response to rejection of the first TA.

Section 2 — Definitions

Assessment: Improvement, but limited

The clearest improvement is the holiday language. The rejected TA reduced the annual holiday-pay cap to five and struck some international-domicile holiday lists. The proposed TA restores the cap to six and restores those international holiday listings. That is a real gain, though concentrated in a relatively narrow definitional area.

Section 3 — General

Assessment: Mostly unchanged, slightly negative in one visible respect

Most of this section appears substantially carried forward. The most notable visible loss is the apparent disappearance of the physical mailbox provision that existed in the rejected TA. That is not a marquee issue, but it is a concrete example of the second TA not simply adding gains everywhere.

Section 4 — Compensation

Assessment: Mixed, and more complicated than a summary would suggest

This is the section most likely to matter to United readers.

Base rates

The proposed TA modestly improves junior and some mid-scale checkpoints, but for much of the senior half of the scale it produces a front-loaded gain / later-checkpoint loss pattern. That means some steps are slightly better early but slightly worse at comparable later checkpoints than in the rejected TA.

Premium and override pay

There does not appear to be a meaningful premium-pay improvement. FSL, Purser, galley, language, reserve override, short-crew, drafting, and drug/alcohol-testing pay all appear substantially unchanged between the rejected and proposed TAs.

Vacation pay

This remains one of the most important negative differences. The rejected TA would have paid vacation at 3:30 immediately. The proposed TA delays 3:30 until the January 2031 bid period.

Overall read

The accurate framing is not “the second TA simply pays more.” It is: the second TA improves parts of the straight scale, extends the scale one more year, but does not improve premium structure and materially weakens near-term vacation-pay value by delaying 3:30 until 2031.

Section 5 — Expenses, Transportation and Lodging

Assessment: Mixed to slightly negative

The proposal preserves the headline per diem levels, but weakens the future per diem escalator and raises the downtown or downtown-like hotel threshold from 17 hours to 19 hours. Improved crew-meal language appears to remain. This is not a clean second-TA improvement story.

Section 6 — Minimum Pay and Credit, Hours of Service, and Contractual Legalities

Assessment: Mixed, with major implementation-delay implications

The broad duty/rest structure largely carries forward, and important pieces are delayed into later implementation windows. This is also where the unchanged drafting premium remains a continuing weak point.

Section 7 — Scheduling

Assessment: Modified, but not fundamentally rebuilt

There are real edits in scheduling, particularly around notifications, reassignment, personal drops, and implementation mechanics. But the structure still looks more like targeted revision than a clean redesign. Much depends on implementation timing.

Section 8 — Reserve Scheduling Procedures

Assessment: Mixed, and less dramatic than summaries may imply

Much of the core reserve structure remains familiar, but some process improvements depend on implementation rather than immediate day-one effect. The most meaningful analytical issue here is how the proposed RAP system compares with better reserve systems elsewhere, especially Southwest.

Section 9 — Special Qualification Flight Attendants

Assessment: Limited improvement

There are programmatic changes, especially around International Purser timing and evaluation, but premium values themselves do not meaningfully improve. This is more administrative refinement than economic breakthrough.

Section 10 — AMC Operation

Assessment: Mostly unchanged in core structure, but with immediate implementation items

The AMC/CRAF framework appears largely intact, but the implementation agreement shows several DOS items involving CRAF override, aeromedical pay, death/disability items, and staffing/order mechanics. This reads as refinement, not redesign.

Section 11 — Training & General Meetings

Assessment: Improvement, but partly delayed

Training protections improve in several areas, including future removal of the 5-hour deadhead cap and stronger rest-treatment rules, but many of the meaningful gains are delayed into later bid periods.

Section 12 — Vacations

Assessment: Mixed

The proposal keeps several structural vacation improvements, including buyback and flex-related features, but the delayed 3:30 vacation pay continues to overshadow much of the section’s positive architecture.

Section 13 — Sick Leave

Assessment: Improvement

The proposal raises the sick-bank maximum and occupational injury bank maximum. Some of this is delayed, but it is still one of the clearer substantive gains in the TA.

Section 14 — Seniority

Assessment: No material change visible

No meaningful change surfaced here. The proposal does not appear to use seniority as a major response area to the first rejection.

Section 15 — Leaves of Absence

Assessment: Improvement

This is one of the stronger sections in the proposal. Paid maternity leave, paid parental leave, paid adoption leave, and return-to-work line protections are among the clearest employee-facing gains.

Section 16 — Job Share and Partnership Flying Programs

Assessment: Mostly continuity, with some refinement

The structure remains largely intact, though at least one line-division item is effective at date of signing. This is more program maintenance than headline gain.

Section 17 — Filling of Vacancies

Assessment: Modest improvement / refinement

The implementation agreement flags vacancy COMAT and recall items for date-of-signing implementation. Important for affected readers, but not a major contract-wide headline.

Section 18 — Reduction in Personnel

Assessment: Largely unchanged

The same basic furlough / voluntary mitigation structure appears to remain in place. No major visible new protection or deterioration surfaced.

Section 19 — Safety, Health and Security

Assessment: Improvement

This is one of the stronger non-economic sections. Safety-representative access, crisis-response participation, irregular-ops reporting access, and related process items improve at date of signing.

Section 20 — Medical Examinations

Assessment: No major visible change

This section appears largely carried forward.

Section 21 — Alcohol and Drug Testing

Assessment: Largely unchanged

The basic framework appears carried forward, and testing pay remains unchanged.

Section 22 — Personnel Files

Assessment: Improvement

This is a meaningful improvement section. Counseling duration and Performance Warning 2 duration are improved, and occupational absences are better protected from generating attendance points, though some of these changes are phased rather than immediate.

Section 23 — Investigations & Grievances

Assessment: Improvement

The proposal strengthens investigative notice and grievance-process cadence, including 48-hour notice before investigative meeting and quarterly grievance-setting or withdrawal meetings. This is one of the stronger enforcement-architecture sections.

Section 24 — System Board of Adjustment

Assessment: Improvement

The arbitrator panel grows from 11 to 13, with scheduled-hearing improvements at date of signing. That is a real institutional gain for enforceability and throughput.

Section 25 — Uniforms

Assessment: Improvement

United taking on first-uniform and luggage startup cost, plus related allotment improvements, is a concrete gain. Some replacement or access items are delayed, but the section is clearly more favorable overall.

Section 26 — Moving Expenses

Assessment: Improvement

The expense cap rises to 20,500 at date of signing. Narrow, but clearly favorable.

Section 27 — Missing, Interned, Hostage or Prisoner of War

Assessment: No material change visible

No major change surfaced.

Section 28 — Commuter Program

Assessment: Mixed

The ability to use offline flights to qualify is a meaningful gain, but another visible commuter-related hotel limitation tempers the section.

Section 29 — Benefits

Assessment: Improvement, but with an important caveat on retiree medical

This is a meaningful gain area in matching and retiree-medical extension. But the retiree-medical sunset was not removed. It appears to have been extended, not cured.

Section 30 — Union Activities

Assessment: Improvement

Enhanced CCS access at MEC and LEC levels strengthens representation capacity and practical enforcement.

Section 31 — Union Security and Check-Off

Assessment: No major visible change

The basic dues/checkoff structure appears stable.

Section 32 — Duration

Assessment: Mixed in practical effect

The length of the proposed TA matters largely because value is heavily shaped by phased implementation. A longer TA with delayed gains changes how members should evaluate headline improvements.

Special issue analysis

1. Compensation by seniority step

The compensation story is more complicated than “the second TA pays more.”

At the junior end, the proposed TA generally does better. At the senior half of the scale, many steps show a pattern of being slightly better early and slightly worse later at comparable checkpoints. This is especially visible from roughly the seventh year upward.

The premium structure remains a major weakness. FSL, Purser, language, galley, reserve override, short-crew, drafting, and testing pay do not appear meaningfully improved.

Report takeaway: The proposal improves parts of the straight scale, but not the premium architecture, and it does so in a way that is more favorable to junior and some mid-scale flight attendants than to many senior-scale flight attendants.

2. Retiree medical benefit extension

This is one of the issues of greatest interest to senior flight attendants.

The retiree-medical sunset was not removed. It was extended. That is a real gain, but not a permanent fix. Flight attendants retiring after the extended window still fall outside the more favorable protected retiree-medical structure and into the lesser fallback arrangement.

Report takeaway: The proposal did not solve the retiree-medical sunset problem. It postponed it.

3. Section 7.Q reassignment — what it actually does

This is one of the most misunderstood and contentious parts of the United contract.

The basic structure is:

  • after 1600 local the day before the lost trip, the flight attendant may contact Scheduling to ask if reassignment is likely
  • if remaining subject to reassignment, the flight attendant must contact Scheduling between 1800 and 2200 local
  • Scheduling must then either assign or release the flight attendant without loss of pay

The reassignment limits matter:

  • no more than two hours earlier than the lost trip’s scheduled departure
  • no interference with the next scheduled calendar day off without consent
  • if a day off is used by consent, there is restoration plus premium pay
  • late-return reassignment premiums matter and should be specifically described in the published version

Important subsection: when a lineholder blocks in illegal for the next day

The contract does not cleanly say that legal rest automatically attaches at block-in such that no contact is required or permitted. Instead, when a flight attendant becomes ineligible for the next scheduled trip, they must notify Scheduling as soon as possible upon return to base from the trip that caused the ineligibility, and at that point they become subject to reassignment under Section 7.Q.

That is one reason reassignment remains controversial. The contract imposes a duty to call, but does not clearly state that this obligation is deferred until after rest.

Important subsection: why missed 7.Q contact can mean lost pay but no points

This is another real-world inconsistency that the contract does not meaningfully resolve.

Section 7.Q.6 creates the obligation to notify Scheduling. But the attendance and discipline section does not clearly map a 7.Q.6 violation onto a specific point-generating occurrence. The listed attendance events are things like illness-related reporting failures, DNF, late check-in, late boarding, missed meeting or training, and similar occurrences. There is no clean category that says “failed to call under 7.Q.6.”

That drafting gap helps explain why the real-world outcome can be:

  • illegal next-day pairing drops
  • loss of pay attaches
  • no attendance or performance point necessarily attaches

Report takeaway: The contract creates a reassignment obligation without clearly defining the disciplinary consequence for breach. That is poor drafting and helps explain why 7.Q remains a perennial source of conflict between flight attendants and Scheduling.

Letters of Agreement and side-letter architecture

Why the LOAs matter

The concern about LOAs is validated by the documents.

The rejected TA contains more LOAs than the proposed TA, but the essential point is not the count. It is the function. These are not marginal appendices. They carry implementation timing, grievance support, safety investigation structure, payment mechanics, and operational rules that materially affect how the TA works in practice. A reader who skips them will not fully understand the deal.

LOA 9 — Implementation Agreement

This is the single most important LOA.

It tells readers:

  • what is effective at date of signing
  • what is delayed into 2026, 2027, 2028, or 2031
  • what remains subject to implementation work rather than immediate exercise

Without LOA 9, a member could easily overestimate the near-term value of the TA.

The old Section 7.Q.5 letter

The rejected TA includes a standalone LOA titled Section 7.Q.5. That means a major reassignment-related settlement rule sat outside the numbered contract in letter form. In the proposed TA, that standalone LOA disappears from the LOA list and the numbering shifts. That strongly suggests the old settlement-letter logic was absorbed into the body text rather than left as a separate letter.

This is a perfect example of why United flight attendants often experience LOAs as obscure or under-read even when they are operationally very important.

Other important LOAs

The proposed TA’s LOAs also include items like:

  • joint grievance training
  • safety investigations
  • implementation of new technology
  • one-time payment mechanics

These are not automatically bad. Some are clearly positive. But they are still essential reading.

Report takeaway: The LOAs are not optional reading. They are a second layer of contract architecture.

Industry benchmark section

Why this benchmark matters

Many United flight attendants judge the proposed TA not only against the rejected TA, but also against the contracts they believe define the industry standard. The two comparators raised most often are Southwest and Alaska.

The report should make two methodological points before comparing numbers:

  • United uses an hourly system.
  • Southwest and Alaska use TFP systems.

That means no simple apples-to-apples pay-table comparison exists.

Standalone comparator: Southwest compensation benchmark

The most accurate summary is:

The widely held view that Southwest flight attendants are among the best paid in the industry is substantially credible if “best paid” means the strongest overall compensation architecture rather than the single highest headline table number.

Southwest’s strength comes not only from straight pay, but from:

  • strong reserve economics
  • clearer premium consequences for scheduling overreach
  • holiday and red-eye monetization
  • broader premium structure
  • strong retirement contribution

This does not mean Southwest necessarily wins every headline pay-table comparison, because the pay units differ. But it does mean Southwest remains one of the strongest benchmarks for total compensation quality.

Standalone comparator: United reserve system vs. Southwest reserve system

United’s proposed RAP system is a real improvement over a less structured reserve model and over the rejected TA in at least two respects.

But United still falls short of the stronger Southwest model. Southwest uses fixed reserve contact windows and tighter assignment boundaries beyond those windows, with explicit premium consequences for overreach.

Report takeaway: Southwest remains the stronger reserve-quality benchmark.

Standalone comparator: Alaska compensation benchmark

Alaska should be included as a second comparator because a meaningful share of readers point to Alaska as the true industry leader in compensation.

Why Alaska is analytically useful

Alaska’s own contract effectively acknowledges the same apples-to-apples problem we have been discussing. Alaska pays in TFP, not block hours, and its CBA expressly provides “block hour equivalents” for industry comparison purposes only, while stating that Alaska flight attendants are not paid in block hours.

That becomes even more important because Alaska’s LOA 16 Market Rate Adjustment provides a useful roadmap for how Alaska itself approaches cross-carrier comparisons. That LOA confirms that cross-carrier pay comparisons are structurally difficult and require explicit conversion assumptions and judgment calls.

Alaska first-pass compensation points to include

  • Alaska’s base TFP table for 2025–2027 runs to strong top-of-scale numbers in Years 13 and 14
  • Alaska also publishes estimated with-boarding-pay ranges, showing that the same pay step can vary substantially depending on actual flying and number of boardings
  • Alaska layers in longevity premiums beginning at 16 years
  • Alaska’s retirement section includes a strong 401(k) match and true-up structure

Comparative conclusion

The report should not say, flatly, “Southwest is the undisputed leader” or “Alaska is the true leader” without qualification.

The stronger and more defensible statement is: Southwest and Alaska both have serious claims to industry-leading compensation, but they get there through different contract architectures, and neither can be compared cleanly to United’s hourly system without explicit conversion assumptions.

Recommended published framing

  • Southwest: strongest reserve-and-premium monetization benchmark
  • Alaska: strongest TFP-system comparator for readers who want to test “best paid” claims against another CWA-AFA contract with explicit market-comparison logic built into the agreement itself

What summary materials may emphasize — and what may be missed

Likely emphasized

  • improved top-line pay table presentation
  • paid leave gains
  • sick-bank increases
  • selected safety and grievance gains
  • benefits matching and retiree-medical extension
  • reserve modernization through RAPs

Likely missed or softened

  • senior-scale later-checkpoint hourly deterioration relative to the rejected TA
  • unchanged premium position pay
  • delayed 3:30 vacation pay
  • the fact that many improvements are phased
  • the fact that retiree medical sunset is extended, not removed
  • the fact that RAPs still do not approach Southwest’s reserve model
  • the full significance of the Implementation Agreement
  • the continuing ambiguity of Section 7.Q

Decision framework for readers

Reasons a reader might vote yes

  • the TA does contain real gains and is better than the rejected TA in several meaningful areas
  • paid leave, sick leave, benefits matching, safety access, and grievance/System Board infrastructure are not cosmetic improvements
  • some members may conclude that the cumulative value of those gains outweighs the continued weaknesses

Reasons a reader might vote no

  • many senior readers may conclude the proposal remains structurally underwhelming where they most care: premium economics, retiree-medical permanence, and reassignment clarity
  • members who expected rejection of the first TA to produce a more fundamental reset may see mostly targeted revision rather than a rebuilt agreement
  • members who focus on near-term value may place significant weight on delayed implementation and delayed vacation pay

The fairest closing formulation: The proposed TA is better than the rejected TA in important ways, but it still leaves several of the most contentious economic and quality-of-life issues only partly resolved. For some readers, especially those focused on reserve, reassignment, premium structure, and retiree medical, the remaining weaknesses may be dispositive. For others, the cumulative gains in leave, benefits, safety, and enforcement may be enough to support ratification.

First-pass bottom line

The proposed 2026–2031 TA is a mixed agreement with real gains, real weaknesses, and a large amount of value that depends on delayed implementation rather than immediate delivery. It is stronger than the rejected TA in several meaningful respects, but it does not fully resolve the concerns that most animate skeptical United flight attendants — especially on senior-scale economics, premium structure, retiree medical permanence, and reassignment clarity.
