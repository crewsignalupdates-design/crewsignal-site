Rejected 2025–2030 TA vs. Proposed 2026–2031 TA

Rejected 2025–2030 TA vs. Proposed 2026–2031 TA

Editor’s Note: This report compares the rejected 2025–2030 tentative agreement with the proposed 2026–2031 tentative agreement section by section, then places the proposed agreement against the two comparators most often cited by United readers: Southwest and Alaska. The goal is not to advocate for a yes or no vote, but to identify what materially changed, what did not, what may be overstated in summary materials, and what readers should understand before deciding how to vote.

Overview

This comparison separates four questions that are often blurred together in ratification summaries: what changed in the text, what changed in actual economic value or daily working life, what is immediate versus delayed, and what remains weaker than the rejected deal even where the new TA improved other areas.

The most important methodological rule is simple: the Letters of Agreement are part of the contract architecture, not optional appendices. A reader who does not parse the letters — especially the Implementation Agreement — will not fully understand what this TA actually delivers and when.

Executive summary

The proposed 2026–2031 TA is not a wholesale rebuild of the rejected 2025–2030 TA. It is better understood as a targeted revision: some sections show real improvement, some mostly carry forward the rejected TA’s structure, and a few important economic items remain weaker than the rejected deal or are delayed far enough into the agreement that their near-term value is materially reduced.

Most accurate one-sentence read: The proposed TA is neither the disaster some members may fear nor the comprehensive fix some summaries may imply. It is a mixed agreement with genuine gains, genuine weaknesses, and a large amount of value that depends on delayed implementation rather than immediate delivery.

Strongest arguments in favor

  • Selected base-rate improvements, especially at junior and some mid-scale checkpoints.
  • Real gains in paid leave, sick-bank capacity, benefits matching, and selected safety-process access.
  • Stronger grievance and System Board infrastructure.
  • Some real reserve modernization through RAPs, even if still short of best-in-class.

Strongest arguments against

  • Many senior-scale pay checkpoints are slightly worse later than in the rejected TA.
  • Premium position pay was not meaningfully improved.
  • 3:30 vacation pay was delayed until January 2031 instead of being immediate.
  • Many improvements are phased in as late as 2027–2031.
  • Retiree medical sunset was extended, not eliminated.
  • Section 7.Q reassignment remains dense, contentious, and imperfectly drafted.

Section-by-section first look

Section 4 — Compensation (Rejected TA) / Section 4 — Compensation (Proposed TA)

This is the section most likely to matter to United readers. The proposal modestly improves junior and some mid-scale checkpoints, but for much of the senior half of the scale it produces a front-loaded gain / later-checkpoint loss pattern. The premium structure remains underwhelming, and the delayed 3:30 vacation-pay implementation remains one of the most important negatives in the new deal.

Section 8 — Reserve (Rejected TA) / Section 8 — Reserve (Proposed TA)

Much of the core reserve structure remains familiar, but the proposed RAP system is a real change. It is better than a looser all-day reserve model and better than the rejected TA in some respects, but it still falls short of the stronger Southwest model discussed later in this report.

Section 12 — Vacations (Rejected TA) / Section 12 — Vacations (Proposed TA)

The proposal preserves several structural vacation improvements, but the delayed 3:30 vacation-pay implementation continues to overshadow much of the section’s positive architecture.

Section 15 — Leaves of Absence (Proposed TA)

This is one of the stronger improvement sections. Paid maternity leave, paid parental leave, paid adoption leave, and return-to-work line protections are among the clearest employee-facing gains.

Sections 22–24 — Personnel Files, Investigations & Grievances, System Board (Proposed TA)

This is one of the more important improvement clusters in the proposal. Counseling and warning durations improve, investigative notice strengthens, grievance cadence improves, and the arbitrator panel expands from 11 to 13.

Sections 25–32 — Uniforms through Duration (Proposed TA)

The clearest positives here are first-uniform/luggage coverage, moving-expense cap improvement, benefits matching, retiree-medical extension, and better CCS access for representation. The most important caveat is that retiree medical was extended, not permanently fixed.

Special issue explainers

Retiree Medical Sunset: extension is not elimination

The proposed TA does not remove the retiree-medical sunset. It extends the protected window from fifteen years to sixteen years from August 28, 2016. That one-year extension is real, but it does not eliminate the underlying structure: flight attendants who retire after the sunset expires are no longer eligible for retiree medical under the more favorable protected provisions and instead fall into the less favorable fallback arrangement.

In practical terms, this can pressure older flight attendants nearing retirement to retire sooner than they otherwise would, because delaying retirement too long may cost them access to the better retiree-medical category.

Bridge Medical and Sick-Bank Depletion

For the retiree bridge-medical group, accrued sick leave appears to function as a retiree-health asset. Participation is tied to having enough sick leave to fund coverage at fourteen hours per month. If the retiree lacks sufficient sick leave, they may still participate only at the unsubsidized regular retiree rate.

This appears to operate as a monthly drawdown, not an upfront lump prepayment. But the bank that funds those monthly deductions appears fixed at retirement. The contract does not indicate that new sick leave continues to accrue after retirement for this purpose.

In practical terms, that creates a strong incentive for flight attendants nearing retirement to conserve sick leave while still active, because using too much sick leave before retirement may reduce or eliminate the economic value of bridge medical coverage later.

Section 7.Q Reassignment Explained

Section 7.Q remains one of the most misunderstood and contested parts of the agreement. The core structure is that, after a lost trip or ineligibility event, the lineholder may remain subject to reassignment within a defined framework. But the legal text, operational practice, and old side-letter history have combined to make this one of the least intuitive parts of the contract.

The contract does not cleanly say that legal rest automatically attaches at block-in such that no contact is required or permitted. Instead, when a flight attendant becomes ineligible for the next scheduled trip, they must notify Scheduling as soon as possible upon return to base from the trip that caused the ineligibility, and at that point they become subject to reassignment.

Why missed 7.Q contact can mean lost pay but no points

Section 7.Q.6 creates the obligation to notify Scheduling. But the attendance and discipline sections do not clearly map a 7.Q.6 violation onto a specific point-generating occurrence. The result is a drafting gap: the illegal next-day pairing may drop with loss of pay, but no attendance or performance point necessarily attaches.

The agreement therefore creates an obligation without clearly defining the disciplinary consequence for breach.

LOAs are not optional reading

The LOAs are not marginal appendices. They carry implementation timing, grievance support, safety investigation structure, payment mechanics, and operational rules that materially affect how the TA works in practice. A reader who skips them will not fully understand the deal.

Industry benchmarks: Southwest and Alaska

Many United flight attendants judge the proposed TA not only against the rejected TA, but also against the contracts they believe define the industry standard. The two comparators raised most often are Southwest and Alaska.

Methodology note: hourly versus TFP is not apples-to-apples

United uses an hourly pay system. Southwest and Alaska use TFP systems. That means no simple headline table comparison is sufficient. Alaska’s own contract explicitly acknowledges this problem by publishing block-hour equivalents for comparison purposes only and by setting out a market-rate-adjustment formula for comparing Alaska against hourly carriers and Southwest.

Southwest benchmark

Southwest remains the strongest reserve-quality benchmark. It uses fixed reserve contact windows and tighter assignment boundaries beyond those windows, with explicit premium consequences for overreach. Its compensation system also monetizes reserve, holiday work, red-eye flying, and retirement more aggressively than the proposed United TA.

Alaska benchmark

Alaska is the most useful second comparator because it is also a CWA-AFA contract using a TFP system, yet Alaska’s own agreement explicitly acknowledges the difficulty of cross-carrier pay comparisons. Alaska’s Section 21 publishes block-hour equivalents for comparison purposes only, and LOA 16 sets out a market-rate-adjustment formula that compares Alaska to United, American, Delta, Southwest, and JetBlue with defined conversion rules.

That makes Alaska especially useful for showing readers that industry-leading compensation claims often depend on methodology, not just the top line of a pay table.

Retiree medical comparator: United vs. Southwest vs. Alaska

Before publication, readers should understand that United appears unusual among these three comparators in having a true calendar-window retiree-medical sunset. Under the proposed United TA, the protected retiree-medical categories remain subject to a fixed historical window tied to August 28, 2016, and the new TA extends that window by one year rather than removing it.

Southwest does not appear to use the same kind of fixed-date sunset structure. Its retiree-health provisions are restrictive and depend on age, service, and sick-leave thresholds, but not on the same sort of historical closing window that pushes later retirees out of a protected retiree-medical class.

Alaska does have a sunset-like feature, but it is structurally different. Alaska’s LOA 18 Retiree Health Insurance is a one-time CBA-bound retiree-health arrangement that runs with the 2025–2028 agreement and expires with the subsequent agreement. That is a temporary program sunset, not the same kind of protected-class retiree-medical cutoff used in the United TA.

For purposes of this report, the comparison is confined to United, Southwest, and Alaska. A broader industry review may be worth a separate follow-up analysis.

LOA appendix: why the letters matter

The Letters of Agreement are a second layer of contract architecture. They govern implementation timing, training, safety investigations, technology, payment mechanics, and other rights and obligations that materially affect how the TA functions in practice.

The most important letter is the Implementation Agreement (LOA 9). It tells readers what is DOS, what is delayed into later bid periods, and what remains dependent on later implementation work.

What summary materials may emphasize — and what may be missed

Likely emphasized

  • Improved top-line pay table presentation.
  • Paid leave gains.
  • Sick-bank increases.
  • Selected safety and grievance gains.
  • Benefits matching and retiree-medical extension.
  • Reserve modernization through RAPs.

Likely missed or softened

  • Senior-scale later-checkpoint hourly deterioration relative to the rejected TA.
  • Unchanged premium position pay.
  • Delayed 3:30 vacation pay.
  • The fact that many improvements are phased.
  • The fact that retiree medical sunset is extended, not removed.
  • The full significance of LOA 9.
  • The continuing ambiguity of Section 7.Q.

Decision framework for readers

Reasons a reader might vote yes

The TA does contain real gains and is better than the rejected TA in several meaningful areas. Paid leave, sick leave, benefits matching, safety access, and grievance/System Board infrastructure are not cosmetic improvements. Some members may conclude that the cumulative value of those gains outweighs the continued weaknesses.

Reasons a reader might vote no

Many senior readers may conclude the proposal remains structurally underwhelming where they most care: premium economics, retiree-medical permanence, and reassignment clarity. Members who expected rejection of the first TA to produce a more fundamental reset may see mostly targeted revision rather than a rebuilt agreement. Members who focus on near-term value may place significant weight on delayed implementation and delayed vacation pay.

Fairest closing formulation: The proposed TA is better than the rejected TA in important ways, but it still leaves several of the most contentious economic and quality-of-life issues only partly resolved. For some readers, especially those focused on reserve, reassignment, premium structure, and retiree medical, the remaining weaknesses may be dispositive. For others, the cumulative gains in leave, benefits, safety, and enforcement may be enough to support ratification.
