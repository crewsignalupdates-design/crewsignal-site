Current CBA vs. TA1 vs. TA2 Comparative Analysis

Current agreement effective August 28, 2016 · Amendable since August 28, 2021

Comparison frame: current 2016–2021 amendable agreement vs. rejected 2025–2030 TA vs. proposed 2026–2031 TA

Editor’s Note: This analysis is a contract-architecture comparison. It uses a dual frame throughout: TA2 versus TA1 and TA2 versus the current 2016–2021 operating baseline. That dual frame matters because TA2’s implementation language makes clear that, until a provision is implemented, Flight Attendants continue operating under the current amendable agreement.

Overview

The proposed 2026–2031 TA is best understood as a targeted revision rather than a fundamental reset. Some areas are materially better. Some largely carry forward prior tentative-agreement structure. Some remain structurally underwhelming. And several of the most important issues remain only partly resolved.

Methodology

This report clarifies four often-conflated issues: changes to text versus day-to-day value, immediate versus delayed effects, and persistent weaknesses. Critically, the Letters of Agreement are not optional appendices; they are the foundational architecture that defines how the contract actually functions.

The second methodological rule is equally important: this report avoids assigning bargaining motives without documentary evidence. Regarding scope or TFP, it identifies the contractual effects and resulting operational advantages rather than speculating on subjective intent.

Section 1 — Recognition, Successorship and Mergers

Current CBA p.4TA1 p.4TA2 p.4

Status: No material change in the body text.

Across the current 2016–2021 amendable agreement, the rejected 2025–2030 TA, and the proposed 2026–2031 TA, the core Section 1 architecture remains substantively stable. Recognition language, successorship protections, McCaskill-Bond seniority integration, separate operations before integrated lists and post-merger rates/rules/working conditions, no-furlough-before-integration, and expedited arbitration all remain in place. TA2 does not use the body of Section 1 as the place for a large new merger-protection response to the rejection of TA1.

Why it matters: That matters because readers should not confuse a stable Section 1 with a stable scope regime overall. The real scope development sits outside the body text, in TA2’s Scope LOA 16.

Assessment: No material change in the body text; the consequential issue is in LOA 16, not Section 1 itself.

Adjunct — Scope Alert / LOA 16

Current CBA Scope LOA 25 p.404TA1 Scope LOA 16 p.436TA2 Scope LOA 16 p.407

TA2 adds the most consequential new scope language in the entire package. The current 2016–2021 CBA and TA1 both use an older model: United will not establish or purchase an alter-ego airline, and if it establishes or purchases a commuter airline (primary 135 carrier), AFA must be recognized and the parties must negotiate an area-standard agreement for that carrier’s flight attendants. TA2 keeps that structure and then adds something materially new: a United Express regional-carrier carve-out.

Under TA2’s Scope LOA 16, United may create or acquire a controlling interest in and control of a regional carrier conducting United Express flying. Flights of that carrier may be staffed by that carrier’s own flight attendants unless the flights are operated by United pilots covered by the United pilot agreement, in which case they must be staffed by United flight attendants. That opening does not appear in the current CBA and did not appear in TA1.

What can be said without speculating about motive

The contracts do not reveal management’s subjective motive, and this report does not assign one. What can be said confidently is functional: this change gives United a clearer contractual path to structure future United Express flying through a controlled regional-carrier model without requiring that all such flying be staffed by United system flight attendants. In practical terms, that is added structural flexibility for the carrier.

Section 2 — Definitions

Current CBA p.7TA1 p.7TA2 p.7

Status: Selective improvement, but largely carry-forward from TA1.

The clearest definitional improvement is the holiday language. The current amendable contract caps paid holidays at five. TA1 raises that structure to six and restores or broadens holiday language for international domiciles, and TA2 preserves that broader structure. Most of the other core scheduling and pay-related definitions appear substantially carried forward.

Why it matters: Definitions are never cosmetic if they govern later sections. Here, the real gain is not that TA2 newly solved the issue relative to TA1; it is that TA2 preserves a real improvement over the current operating baseline.

Assessment: Improvement relative to the current CBA; largely preservation of a TA1 gain, not a new TA2 breakthrough.

Section 3 — General

Current CBA p.13TA1 p.14TA2 p.13

Status: Mostly unchanged, with one visible regression.

Most of Section 3 appears substantially carried forward. Jumpseat rules, reciprocal cabin seat structure, pass-travel framework, deadhead and general operational provisions do not show a major rewrite. Section 4 — Compensation

Current CBA p.31TA1 p.38TA2 p.35

Status: Mixed and structurally important.

Section 4 is where TA2 most clearly tries to respond to the rejection of TA1, but the answer is only partial. The package shows selected front-end improvements at junior and some mid-scale checkpoints, while many senior-scale readers will still see weaker later-step economics than they expected. Premium position economics do not show a comparable breakthrough. TA2 does, however, add sit pay, and that should be treated as a real new compensation item rather than a summary-level re-labeling.

Why it matters: This is where summary materials can flatten the real issue. A contract can pay more at some visible steps and still compare less favorably in premium structure, vacation timing, reserve monetization, or long-run total compensation. TA2 improves some base-rate presentation, but it still delays the 3:30 vacation-pay value until the January 2031 bid period and leaves the premium structure largely intact.

Assessment: Real front-end gains, real new sit pay, but still a mixed compensation section because value remains uneven and back-loaded.

Methodology note — hourly versus TFP

United uses an hourly structure. Southwest and Alaska use TFP-style trip-pay systems. That means headline pay-table comparisons are not apples-to-apples. Alaska is the cleanest methodology example because Alaska’s own CBA publishes block-hour-equivalent figures for comparison purposes and uses a market-rate-adjustment formula to convert hourly-carrier scales into TFP logic.

What advantage does a carrier get from TFP? Without speculating about motive, the structural advantage is clear: a TFP system prices a trip as a production unit rather than paying only raw block time. That lets management blend block, boarding, sit, turn, and trip-shape assumptions into one model and then benchmark more easily across carriers with different work-rule architectures. Whether that is better for workers depends entirely on the surrounding overrides, reserve monetization, rigs, and retirement structure — not on the existence of TFP by itself.

Section 5 — Expenses, Transportation and Lodging

Current CBA p.39TA1 p.50TA2 p.47

Status: Improvement relative to the current baseline; mixed only in one hotel refinement.

TA2 preserves the larger per diem economics and the stronger hotel-gainshare structure that already appeared in TA1. Relative to the current contract, the move from a 19-hour to a 17-hour downtown / downtown-like hotel threshold remains a real gain. The one mixed comparison point is the hotel room-not-ready trigger: TA1 used the originally scheduled arrival, while TA2 returns to an arrival-based trigger with an early-arrival offset.

Why it matters: This section affects daily quality of life and out-of-pocket exposure. It is stronger than a quick read suggests, but the fairest read is still that TA2 mostly preserves gains already won in TA1 rather than materially expanding them.

Assessment: Improvement over the current CBA; mostly TA1-to-TA2 preservation, with a mixed hotel-timing refinement.

Section 6 — Minimum Pay and Credit, Hours of Service, and Contractual Legalities

Current CBA p.45TA1 p.60TA2 p.55

Status: Important improvement area, but implementation-heavy.

Section 6 remains one of the most important quality-of-life sections in the entire report. The TA architecture shows real duty, rest, and legality changes, but many of the most meaningful items are tied to implementation timing rather than immediate ratification effect. That means TA2 cannot be evaluated on text alone; it must be read with LOA 9.

Why it matters: For readers actually working the current contract, the practical question is not whether Section 6 eventually improves, but how soon. Because implementation is staggered, this section strengthens the pro-ratification case in principle while remaining more back-loaded than the headline story suggests.

Assessment: Improvement relative to the current baseline, but with major timing caveats and less fresh TA2 innovation than summary language may imply.

Section 7 — Scheduling

Current CBA p.59TA1 p.77TA2 p.72

Status: Still one of the decisive sections; improved in places, but not fundamentally reset.

Section 7 remains central because it governs how lineholders actually experience loss of flight time, reassignment, trip protection, and open-flying control. TA2 appears to preserve much of the broader TA1 scheduling architecture, with some targeted refinements, but it does not amount to a wholesale rebuild. The section still leaves dense language around reassignment and practical ambiguity that skeptical readers were already focused on.

Why it matters: Scheduling language determines whether contractual value is usable. Even when pay improves, broad company flexibility or dense loss-of-flight-time rules can materially change how that value is actually experienced. That is why Section 7.Q remains a report centerpiece rather than a side note.

Assessment: Mixed. TA2 keeps major scheduling architecture in place and does not fully resolve the reassignment clarity problem.

Section 8 — Reserve Scheduling Procedures

Current CBA p.91TA1 p.123TA2 p.114

Status: Real improvement over the current baseline, but still below the best-in-class reserve model.

TA2’s RAP-based reserve structure is a real improvement over the older all-day-liability model, and the second TA appears to make narrow but meaningful improvements over TA1 as well. Even so, reserve remains heavily dependent on later implementation and still does not approach the cleaner liability rules and stronger monetization that make Southwest a benchmark in member discussions.

Why it matters: Reserve is a ratification-driving issue because it affects daily control, contact windows, assignment reach, and the pay consequences of overreach. TA2 is better than the current baseline, but it still leaves United with long liability windows, 24-hour availability by preference, and narrower remedies than the strongest comparator models.

Assessment: Meaningful improvement, but still well short of the Southwest standard and still heavily tied to JIT / later implementation.

Adjunct — Cybersecurity policy and discipline risk

This report treats cybersecurity as a management-policy layer, not as a separate negotiated discipline article. The internal policy materials reviewed for this report describe a high-control compliance environment built around monitoring, device control, record retention, and anti-ephemeral-messaging expectations. Because those materials are non-public, this report generalizes the company-policy side and focuses specifically on the contractual impact.

The contractual point is narrower and more important: broader cyber policy may expand the range of company rules, system logs, device-related evidence, and records-retention expectations that appear in an investigation, but it does not create a separate discipline system outside the agreement. Any resulting discipline still runs through the same personnel-file, investigation, grievance, and arbitration machinery discussed in Sections 22, 23, and 24.

Reader takeaway: Cyber policy can widen the evidence base and the kinds of conduct that trigger investigation, but the due-process question remains contractual. That is why this issue belongs next to the safety/security section and then points directly forward into the discipline-and-enforcement sections.

The retiree-medical correction is essential. The current 2016–2021 CBA already contains the sunset. TA1 changes the protected window from fifteen years to sixteen years from August 28, 2016. TA2 keeps that same sixteen-year window. So TA2 does not newly extend the sunset relative to TA1; it preserves the one-year extension already won relative to the current baseline.

The bridge-medical mechanics matter just as much. For the Continental / CMI / new-hire side of the retiree structure, the contract ties bridge participation to the retiree’s sick-leave bank at retirement and funds participation at fourteen hours of sick leave per month. If the retiree does not have enough sick leave, the retiree can continue only at the unsubsidized Regular Retiree Medical rate. That makes unused sick leave a de facto retiree-health asset for the affected group.

Why this matters in plain English

The sunset is not just a date change. It determines whether otherwise-eligible retirees stay in a protected retiree-medical track or fall into the narrower, full-cost fallback structure. That can create a real economic incentive for older Flight Attendants nearing retirement to retire before the protected window closes.

Reader decision framework

Reasons readers may view TA2 favorably

  • Real gains in paid leave, sick-bank capacity, benefits matching, safety access, and enforcement structure.
  • Meaningful improvement over the current operating baseline in reserve modernization, benefits, and multiple quality-of-life areas.
  • A real new sit-pay concept and several date-of-signing items that members can actually feel earlier than the most delayed economics.

Reasons readers may still reject TA2

  • No major premium-pay breakthrough and weaker timing on vacation value than many readers expected.
  • The new Scope LOA 16 carve-out is materially consequential and negative for readers focused on scope protection.
  • Reserve remains below Southwest’s benchmark, the retiree-medical sunset survives, and Section 7.Q remains dense and contentious.
For CrewSignal readers, the most accurate final formulation remains this: TA2 is a mixed agreement with real gains, real weaknesses, and a large amount of value that depends on delayed implementation rather than immediate delivery.
