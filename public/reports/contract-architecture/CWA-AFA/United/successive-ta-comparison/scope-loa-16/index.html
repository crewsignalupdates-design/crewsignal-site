Bottom line: TA2’s LOA 16 is a partial shield, not a full shield. It protects United Flight Attendants strongly when the flying stays on United pilots, but it also creates a new contractual path for controlled United Express regional flying to be staffed by non-mainline Flight Attendants.

The current amendable CBA and the rejected TA1 both use the older three-part scope model: controlled entities may not conduct commercial flight operations historically performed by United Flight Attendants unless they use United-system Flight Attendants; United may not establish or purchase an alter-ego airline; and if United establishes or purchases a commuter airline (primary 135 carrier), AFA is recognized and the parties bargain a competitive agreement no less favorable than area-standard contracts for similar flying.

TA2 keeps those paragraphs and then adds two new pieces. Paragraph 4 says all flights operated by pilots covered by the United pilot agreement and requiring Flight Attendants must be staffed by United Flight Attendants. Paragraph 5 then says United may create or acquire a controlling interest in and control of a regional carrier conducting United Express Flying, and that carrier’s flights may be staffed by that carrier’s own Flight Attendants unless the flights are operated by United pilots, in which case they must be staffed by United Flight Attendants.

The United pilot agreement is why the new language matters so much. Pilot scope gives United pilots the exclusive right to perform Company Flying, but it separately defines and permits United Express Flying. Under Section 1-C-1, United Express flying is regional-aircraft flying on another operating certificate using the United code and United Express or similar branding, or under a revenue-share arrangement. The pilot agreement also says United or a company affiliate may create, acquire, control, manage, take an equity interest in, or transfer aircraft to United Express carriers that comply with Section 1-C-1, and that those operations are not considered Company Flying or Company Aircraft.

That means LOA 16 does not import pilot scope as a full mainline exclusivity clause for Flight Attendants. Instead, it imports the pilot agreement’s regional-flying framework — including its permissions and its fences.

What LOA 16 protects

  • If a flight is operated by pilots covered by the United pilot agreement and requires Flight Attendants, it must be staffed by United Flight Attendants.
  • TA2 imports the pilot agreement’s aircraft, ratio, network, and furlough-related United Express limits rather than leaving the new carve-out completely open-ended.
  • The older alter-ego and commuter-airline recognition language remains in place.

What LOA 16 exposes

  • United may now create or acquire a controlled United Express regional carrier and staff that carrier’s flights with that carrier’s own Flight Attendants unless the flights are operated by United pilots.
  • Protection becomes derivative: where pilot scope places the flying matters to Flight Attendant staffing.
  • Because TA2 imports the pilot agreement’s United Express provisions “as it may be amended,” the outer boundary of the carve-out can move with later pilot-scope amendments.

No-speculation guardrail

The documents support the contractual opening and the pilot-side limits. They do not prove why United wanted the change, whether management plans to use it aggressively, or what outside corporate strategy may follow. The report stays at the contractual level.

Assessment: TA2’s LOA 16 is mixed but leans concessionary from a pure scope perspective. It adds a real staffing protection for flights that remain on United pilots, but it also adds a material new United Express carve-out not found in the current CBA or TA1.
