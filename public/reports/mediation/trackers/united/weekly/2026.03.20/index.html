United / CWA-AFA

Week Ending 2026.03.20

Status Summary

The week ending 2026.03.20 remained a late-stage-but-unfinished mediation week. CrewSignal did not identify a same-week company filing, National Mediation Board process change, or carrier/newswire confirmation that a revised tentative agreement had been completed by March 20.

The only notable non-union/public week-specific signal CrewSignal identified was March 14 third-party reporting centered on a possible scope tradeoff and continued bargaining. That supports a read of active late-stage negotiations, but not a completed package.

In airline labor agreements, scope is the contract language that protects which flying stays with a carrier’s bargaining unit and limits shifting that flying to affiliates, subsidiaries, or lower-cost parallel operators. In plain terms, the United flight-attendant scope issue is about whether flying historically performed by United Flight Attendants could be moved to an alter-ego or controlled affiliate using a different flight-attendant list.

At United, the public current agreement and the public rejected 2025 tentative agreement both preserve the same basic scope framework. They say that if United or a controlled subsidiary conducts commercial flight operations historically performed by United Flight Attendants, that work must be done by Flight Attendants on the United system seniority list. The same language separately says the company will not establish or purchase an alter-ego airline in whole or in part, while preserving a narrower commuter-airline exception that still requires union recognition and bargaining.

That is why this issue is potentially significant. United’s 2025 Form 10-K says the United Express network is important for smaller-city flying that cannot be provided economically with mainline aircraft, that United determines schedules, pricing, and revenue management under its regional capacity-purchase agreements, that it sometimes owns aircraft flown by regional partners, and that it holds material stakes in Republic and CommuteAir. If scope were loosened, United could gain more room to place feeder or future-growth flying into a United-owned or United-controlled regional platform outside the United flight-attendant list. CrewSignal presents that as a possible ramification of a change, not as a confirmed agreed outcome.

Even then, readers should not overstate the consequence. A change to flight-attendant scope would not be a blank check to move all United flying off the mainline overnight. The current United pilot agreement still caps regional aircraft size and counts and limits mileage, hub-to-hub flying, and network shape. So the realistic exposure is at the feeder and future-growth layer, not an immediate transfer of 737, A320, or 787 flying.

The underlying risk is also not theoretical. A 1996 D.C. Circuit opinion arising from UAL’s ownership of Air Wisconsin describes the union demanding that Air Wisconsin flights be staffed by Flight Attendants on the United system seniority list under then-existing scope language. That history does not prove the same outcome now, but it shows that United flight-attendant scope has long mattered when corporate control and regional flying intersect.

  • Scope means job-protection language about who performs covered flying.
  • Potential ramification: a looser scope clause could make feeder or future-growth flying easier to place at a separately staffed affiliate.
  • Important constraint: pilot scope and regional-flying limits would still cabin the size and shape of any such move.
  • Same-week signal: Public discussion focused on possible scope and compensation tradeoffs, not on a finished deal.
  • Documentation gap: No same-week `company or NMB milestone confirmed a revised tentative agreement.
  • Next marker: A documented revised tentative agreement, company statement, or formal NMB process change.

CrewSignal Watch Points

This week matters less for what it proved than for what it still left unproven. A public report suggested the hardest remaining issues may have involved scope, which is consistent with a case that is close but still capable of slipping.

Under the Railway Labor Act, proximity to agreement does not change legal posture by itself. Until a revised tentative agreement is actually documented, United remains an active mediation file rather than a finished settlement.
