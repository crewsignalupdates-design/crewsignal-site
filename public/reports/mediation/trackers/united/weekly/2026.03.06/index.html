Mediation & Negotiation · Weekly Report

United / CWA-AFA

NMB Docket: A-14065 · Mediator: Michael Kelliher

Week Ending 2026.03.06

Status Summary

United and CWA-AFA exited the March 3–6 federal mediation session with the strongest joint forward signal since the July 2025 TA rejection. AFA said the parties made substantial progress, are down to several open issues, and expect to complete a revised tentative agreement at the next session in Washington, DC, on March 24–27. Outside reporting on management messaging pointed in the same direction: the case appears to be in late-stage packaging rather than reset bargaining.

For lineholders, that matters because the dispute remains inside the Railway Labor Act process with no release from mediation and no strike clock running. United’s own 2025 filing confirms the company is still negotiating a revised tentative agreement after recording a major ratification-related charge tied to the rejected deal, reinforcing pressure to finish rather than reopen the economics from scratch. The practical question is whether the parties can convert that framework into a ratifiable TA2 on sit pay, redeye language, scheduling and reserve protections, and other quality-of-life items.

  • March 24–27 session: The next Washington mediation block is now the clearest window for a TA2 announcement.
  • Packaging versus price: Remaining friction appears concentrated in work-rule design and cost offsets, not in whether wages move higher.
  • Escalation posture: Postponing the March 19 action day lowers immediate pressure and signals both sides still see value in finishing within mediation.

CrewSignal Watch Points

After the stronger tone that followed the February 10–12 mediation block, the March 3–6 session in Chicago produced the clearest public convergence yet. AFA said the parties made substantial progress on all outstanding issues, reduced the dispute to several open items, and expect to resolve them at the next federal mediation session in Washington, DC, on March 24–27. Non-union reporting on company communications likewise described management as increasingly confident that a new tentative agreement is close.

The sequencing matters. The May 2025 TA was sold as a major economic catch-up package, but 71% of voting Flight Attendants rejected it in July despite 92% turnout. In United’s 2025 Form 10-K, the carrier said the rejected TA had included scheduling, reserve, and other quality-of-life improvements, recorded a $561 million ratification-related charge, and confirmed that talks continue for a revised TA. That leaves the present round looking less like a restart than a targeted repair effort: core economics appear bounded by the prior deal, while ratification risk turns on whether the remaining work-rule items better match member priorities this time.

  • Mediation track: Still active and structured under NMB oversight; momentum improved, but no release or strike timetable is in play. The next inflection point is whether the March 24–27 Washington session produces a complete TA2 or only another incremental update.
  • TA2 track: Several issues remain, with current reporting pointing to sit pay, redeye language, scheduling and reserve protections, and final economic packaging. The question is whether United can preserve its “top of industry” pay framing while limiting member concerns about hidden offsets or concessions.
  • System-wide: United is managing this case while warning investors that unresolved amendable labor deals can materially raise costs, increasing management’s incentive to reach closure without broadening scope. Watch to see if public messaging shifts from general optimism to specific language on sit pay, redeyes, reserve, hotels, and retro structure and whether postponement of the March 19 action day holds if the March bargaining progress slips.
