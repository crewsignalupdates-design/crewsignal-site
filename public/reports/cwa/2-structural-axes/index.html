Structural Axes: Sectors and Districts

How the Communications Workers of America organizes authority, resources, and representation — and why AFA operates as a national sector outside CWA’s geographic district structure.

1. Why Structure Matters

To understand how AFA operates within the Communications Workers of America (CWA), it is first necessary to understand how CWA organizes itself. CWA does not rely on a single hierarchy. Instead, it uses two intersecting structural axes — industrial sectors and geographic districts — each designed to solve a different organizational problem.

Most CWA members belong to a local that is situated within both an industrial sector and a geographic district. AFA is a notable exception. This report explains why that exception exists and how it was deliberately created.

2. Industrial Sectors: Organizing by Type of Work

Industrial sectors are the primary way CWA groups members according to the work they perform and the industries in which they operate. Sectors exist because bargaining strategy, safety regulation, legal frameworks, and employer behavior vary significantly by industry.

Examples of CWA industrial sectors include telecommunications, media and broadcasting, public sector employment, manufacturing, healthcare, and airlines. Each sector concentrates subject-matter expertise and develops strategies specific to that industry.

Functions of Industrial Sectors

Function Purpose
Bargaining Strategy Coordinate contract negotiations and enforcement across employers within the same industry.
Legal & Regulatory Expertise Develop specialized knowledge of industry-specific labor law and regulatory agencies.
Safety & Standards Address industry-specific safety risks, compliance regimes, and best practices.
Communications & Mobilization Create messaging and organizing strategies relevant to a particular workforce.

For flight attendants, the relevant industrial sector is the airline sector, operated within CWA by AFA–CWA.

3. Geographic Districts: Organizing by Region

Geographic districts are the second major axis of CWA’s structure. Districts organize locals by physical location rather than by industry. They exist to manage functions that are inherently regional: administration, governance oversight, political activity, and staff deployment.

CWA’s districts correspond roughly to major regions of the United States (for example, District 1 in the Northeast, District 3 in the Southeast, and District 9 on the West Coast). Each district has elected leadership and professional staff.

Functions of Geographic Districts

Function Purpose
Administrative Oversight Charters, bylaws review, audits, and internal governance compliance.
Political & Legislative Work State and regional lobbying, electoral campaigns, and AFL-CIO coordination.
Regional Staffing Deployment of organizers, representatives, and training resources.
Inter-Local Coordination Support collaboration among locals within the same geographic area.

4. Why AFA Does Not Operate Within CWA’s District System

AFA does not fit neatly into CWA’s geographic district model. Unlike most CWA locals, AFA’s local councils (LECs) are not defined by a single city, state, or region. They are defined by airline operations and flight attendant bases that often span multiple states and multiple CWA districts under a single employer.

Airline bargaining, safety regulation, and contract enforcement are national — and often international — in scope. Placing AFA’s structure inside geographic districts would fragment authority, duplicate oversight, and undermine system-wide bargaining strategies.

Standard CWA Model vs. AFA Model

Model Structural Flow Designed For
Standard CWA Structure Local → Geographic District → Sector → CWA National Regionally anchored workplaces and employers.
AFA–CWA Structure LEC → MEC → AFA International → CWA National National airline systems spanning multiple regions.

During the 2003–2004 affiliation process, AFA and CWA explicitly preserved AFA’s internal governance and bargaining architecture. The result was a deliberate decision to seat AFA as a national industrial sector operating parallel to, rather than inside, the district system.

5. Autonomy, with Constraints

AFA’s position outside the district system does not mean independence from CWA. AFA operates under its own constitution and maintains control over its internal representation, but it remains subject to the CWA Constitution for matters such as trusteeship, convention governance, and affiliation with the AFL-CIO.

This arrangement — an autonomous, industry-specific sector operating within the constitutional framework of a larger international union — is unusual in U.S. labor law and is central to understanding both AFA’s strengths and its limitations.
