CWA Governance Structure, Districts, and Sectors

How the Communications Workers of America is organized, and where AFA–CWA fits within its constitutional structure and decision-making.

Reference article · Living document
Home / CWA & AFA Series / CWA Structure & Governance

1. Overview of the Communications Workers of America (CWA)

The Communications Workers of America (CWA) is a federation of more than 400,000 members in the United States, Canada, and Puerto Rico. It is governed by the CWA Constitution, which delegates authority through three interconnected levels of representation:

  • National – the Convention and Executive Board
  • Geographic – the Districts
  • Industrial – the Sectors or Divisions

The highest authority within the union is the CWA Convention, where delegates from every district and sector vote on constitutional amendments, policy resolutions, and officer elections.

2. Two Structural Axes: Districts and Sectors

CWA operates on two parallel structural axes that intersect at the Executive Board:

Axis Type Leadership Represents
Geographic Districts 1–7 District Vice Presidents Members organized by location
Industrial Sectors Sector Vice Presidents Members organized by industry or occupation

3. CWA Districts

CWA maintains seven geographic districts, each governed by an elected District Vice President who sits on the Executive Board.

  • District 1 — Northeast
  • District 2-13 — Mid-Atlantic
  • District 3 — Southeast
  • District 4 — Midwest
  • District 6 — Southwest
  • District 7 — Mountain States
  • District 9 — Western States & Hawaii

4. CWA Sectors

Sectors — including AFA-CWA — represent occupational groups across all districts.

  • AFA-CWA — Flight Attendants
  • NABET-CWA — Broadcast Technicians
  • IUE-CWA — Industrial Workers
  • TNG-CWA — NewsGuild
  • PHEW-CWA — Public/Health/Education Workers

5. The AFA-CWA Sector

AFA-CWA is a sector within CWA — not a district — and elects its own Sector Vice President who sits on the CWA Executive Board.

  • LECs – Local Councils at individual bases
  • MECs – airline-level governance bodies
  • AFA International – brings together all MECs and interfaces with CWA National

Through this structure, AFA maintains occupational focus for Flight Attendants while participating fully in CWA’s broader governance and strategy.

6. Funding and Dues Flow

Funding and dues flow upward from members and locals to sectors and the national union. A simplified view looks like this:

Level Funding Retention Primary Use
Local Unions / LECs Member dues ~20–25% Local representation, grievance handling, day-to-day operations
Sectors (e.g., AFA-CWA) Per-capita income from locals ~45–50% Industry-wide bargaining, safety work, legal and communications
CWA National Per-capita income from sectors and direct locals ~25–30% Organizing, political work, audits, district and national staff

District offices are funded from the National treasury, not by collecting dues directly from members.

7. Districts vs. Sectors

Districts and sectors are complementary, not hierarchical. One is based on geography, the other on industry or occupation.

Aspect Districts Sectors (e.g., AFA-CWA)
Basis of Representation Region / geography Industry / occupation
Leadership District Vice Presidents Sector Vice Presidents
Seat on Executive Board Yes Yes
Primary Role Regional administration, organizing, political work Industry-specific bargaining and representation
Funding Source Allocations from National treasury Per-capita dues from locals

8. Summary

CWA’s governance design balances sector autonomy with national accountability. AFA-CWA functions as a self-governing sector for Flight Attendants while remaining part of a larger “union of unions” that coordinates resources, strategy, and political power across districts and industries.
