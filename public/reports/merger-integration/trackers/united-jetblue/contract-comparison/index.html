United vs JetBlue

Flight attendant contract architecture comparison (crew-focused)

Informational only; not legal advice. Always defer to the controlling CBA/LOA language.

Why this comparison exists

The United/JetBlue “Blue Sky” collaboration is not a merger, but deeper interoperability often triggers direct peer benchmarking: crews compare pay-credit mechanics, reserve control, schedule quality, and enforcement tools across airlines doing adjacent flying in overlapping markets.

This report summarizes contract structure differences that tend to drive real-life outcomes: what gets paid, how schedules are built and modified, and how rules are enforced.

Source documents

  • United: Flight Attendant Agreement (CWA-AFA), 2016–2021 edition (as provided).
  • JetBlue: Inflight Crewmember agreement/TA document (TWU Local 579) (as provided).

Note: CBAs evolve via amendments and LOAs. Treat this as an architecture comparison, not a real-time pay-rate calculator.

Executive takeaways

  • “What gets paid” is as important as the hourly rate: both contracts include pay protections and add-on pay concepts, but they express them differently (credit protections vs distinct pay adders).
  • Reserve design drives quality-of-life: the “shape” of reserve blocks, call-out rules, and day-off protection tends to dominate member satisfaction even when wages rise.
  • Time-off architecture differs: separate vacation/sick banks vs a PTO-style approach changes how flexibility works in practice and how absenteeism is managed.

Deeper dive: what tends to move outcomes

1) Pay-credit mechanics under disruption

In real-world operations, “how you get paid when the trip breaks” often matters as much as the published rate. Look for: protections tied to original line value, reassignment outcomes, and what counts as compensable duty time.

2) Reserve control and predictability

Reserve systems that limit block length, protect days off, and constrain assignment windows tend to reduce fatigue and improve schedule stability. Reserve disputes often concentrate around notification timing, reassignment sequences, and whether “off days” are genuinely protected.

3) Time-off architecture (PTO vs segmented banks)

PTO-style systems can increase flexibility but can also shift how attendance is incentivized and enforced. Segmented vacation/sick structures can protect illness-specific use but often reduce “general-purpose flexibility.”

4) Enforcement strength

A contract is only as strong as its enforceability: grievance timelines, remedy language, and system-board/arbitration pathways determine whether violations are rare or simply “priced in.”
