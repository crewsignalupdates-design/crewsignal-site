Contract comparison

United vs JetBlue

Flight attendant contract comparison focused on early-scale pay, reserve quality of life, insurance, scheduling protections, and retirement.

Editor’s Note: This page compares the publicly posted contract documents linked below. It does not assume that Blue Sky becomes a merger. Because public contract libraries can lag active bargaining, this page is best read as a structural comparison of the source agreements currently available, not as a substitute for current bargaining updates, grievance guidance, or legal advice.

How to Read This Comparison

  • This page starts with what a junior flight attendant or reserve feels first: the early pay table, the monthly guarantee, how easily days off can be touched, and what happens when operations unravel.
  • It then looks at benefits that are hard to rebuild once lost: healthcare design, employee contribution exposure, and retirement formulas.
  • Only after that does it zoom out to broader alignment questions such as premium-pay philosophy and representation mechanics.

Key Provisions: Side-by-Side Comparison

Topic United (CWA-AFA) JetBlue (TWU) Why it matters
Early-scale pay The public JCBA gives a single base pay table that starts at $26.68 at date of signing, rises to $28.31 in year 2, and reaches $62.00 at 13th year+ before incentive rates are applied. The public JetBlue agreement splits the picture. The 2026 Straight Pay Scale shows $28.72 for months 7–12 and $31.34 at the first anniversary, while the 2026 Premium Pay Scale shows $23.18 for months 1–6 and $26.16 for months 7–12. Headline top-step pay does not tell a junior crewmember what the first year actually feels like. The bottom of the scale and the guarantee matter more to day-to-day survival than the year-13 headline.
How extra pay starts United uses a separate incentive-pay table. Incentive rates apply only after credited block hours exceed 200 hours in a calendar quarter, subject to a cap and a no-incentive limit above 110 hours in a bid month. JetBlue lets IFCs choose between Straight and Premium pay tracks twice each year. IFCs on the Premium Pay Scale receive premium pay for all credit above 70 hours in a bid period, and the agreement also layers in additional pay categories tied to reschedules, junior assignments, airport standby, and other events. United back-loads extra pay into heavy quarter crediting. JetBlue makes the premium question more monthly and assignment-driven. That difference affects how a crewmember experiences “good money” versus simply chasing legal flying.
Reserve minimum / pay floor Reserves are guaranteed 78 hours pay and credit per month, and United adds a $2.00 reserve override for each credited flight hour. Reserves are guaranteed 75 hours per month on the Premium Pay Scale (70 base + 5 premium) or 70 hours per month on the Straight Pay Scale. This is the first real quality-of-life floor. A reserve guarantee is not just a pay number; it determines how much uncertainty a junior crewmember has to absorb before the month even starts.
Reserve assignment and days-off control United groups reserves by days of availability and then orders them by credited time accrued and FIFO. A reserve cannot be assigned into a designated Set block of days off. In other day-off blocks, the company must first look to volunteers and may not assign a reserve into a day off if another legal and available reserve can take the trip. If a reserve is assigned into the first day off, the reserve receives 5:00 add pay. JetBlue publishes next-day reserve assignments by 1800 Base Local Time and uses a more explicitly layered assignment process, including pairing-length matching, FIFO / FOFO tie-breakers, reserve-hour balancing, and seniority. Airport standby is capped at two in a row and six in a bid period, and a reserve generally cannot be assigned airport standby after completing a pairing unless there is a Level 2 or higher IROP, and never after 8 or more duty hours. Both contracts protect reserves, but they do it differently. United protects important off-time blocks and adds pay when off-time is invaded. JetBlue writes more of the sequencing and standby limits directly into the assignment language.
IRROPS / reassignment / day-off encroachment United relies heavily on pay protection and restoration language. Lineholders who involuntarily lose flying keep projected line value subject to reassignment rules. Drafting carries premium compensation, and a flight attendant cannot be drafted if calendar days off cannot be restored to the monthly minimum. If operations drive work deep into a day off, the contract provides restoration options or 5:00 pay and credit in lieu. JetBlue writes more of the disruption fence line into the reassignment article itself. A non-disrupted IFC cannot be reassigned at base unless all legal and available airport standbys have been used. Reassignment into a vacation day is prohibited without concurrence. Removed segments are pay-protected if a reassignment creates illegality. Redeye reassignment restrictions and positive-contact junior-assignment rules further limit how far the company can reach into off-time. This is where “management latitude” becomes real. The two agreements are not only different in generosity; they are different in philosophy about whether the contract limits the move up front or remedies it after the fact.
Insurance and health coverage United’s benefits language is more contract-specific. Core medical plan designs and the Traditional Medical PPO cannot be amended without union consent, and required monthly contributions for major medical options are capped at 20% of projected coverage cost. The contract also provides for quarterly insurance meetings and data sharing. JetBlue guarantees access to health plans (medical / prescription / dental / vision) and insurance plans (disability / life) that are the same or similar as those offered to other JetBlue crewmembers, except pilots. But the posted agreement does not spell out plan-design economics, contribution caps, or change controls at the same level of detail. Insurance fights are usually about stability and cost exposure, not just whether a plan exists. United locks more of that detail into the contract. JetBlue secures access and parity, but with less contract-level specificity.
Retirement and longer-term economics United hard-codes more retirement economics. New hires and rehires are eligible for the 5% direct contribution plus up to a 3% company match structure used for former subsidiary-United flight attendants. The contract also preserves legacy retirement treatment for some populations, including continued participation in CARP or the IAM National Pension Plan where applicable. JetBlue states that eligible IFCs may participate in the company 401(k) plan, and that profit sharing is available if the company provides it to other crewmembers. The posted agreement does not spell out a defined-benefit pension, a fixed company-match formula, or a parallel legacy-pension structure. Retirement is not just a long-service issue. A contract that locks in actual formulas produces much more certainty than one that mainly guarantees plan access.
Representation / integration mechanics United flight attendants are represented by CWA-AFA. JetBlue inflight crewmembers are represented by TWU. Even if economics aligned perfectly, a single-carrier scenario would still trigger a separate representation question. Work-rule alignment and representation are related, but they are not the same problem.

Pay, Reserve Life, and Quality of Life

  • United’s public JCBA is easier to read at the bottom of the pay table because it is one base-rate ladder plus a separate incentive ladder. JetBlue’s junior pay picture is more conditional because the crewmember’s straight-versus-premium election changes the floor.
  • United gives reserves the stronger monthly floor on paper with a 78-hour guarantee plus the reserve override. JetBlue’s reserve floor depends on the pay-scale election: 75 on Premium, 70 on Straight.
  • In a real alignment fight, the most sensitive question would likely not be the top step. It would be whether the earliest years, the reserve guarantee, and reserve lifestyle are actually ratifiable for the people living them.

Insurance and Health Coverage

  • United locks more of the health-coverage architecture into the contract itself. That includes medical-plan change controls, a 20% contribution cap for major medical options, and a recurring process for insurance problem-solving.
  • JetBlue protects access to company health and insurance plans, but the posted agreement does not define the same level of cost-sharing detail, plan-design permanence, or bargaining friction around changes.
  • That makes insurance a bigger alignment issue than it may first appear. A crew can live with two different plan names; it is much harder to live with a weaker contribution cap or less certainty about what can be changed.

Bidding, Scheduling, and IRROPS Protections

  • United protects pay and off-time in meaningful ways, but much of that protection reads as remedy language: keep the line guarantee, restore the day off, pay the add pay, or pay the drafting premium once the schedule move has happened.
  • JetBlue more often writes the limits into the move itself. Its publicly posted agreement is more explicit about next-day reserve processing, airport-standby limits, vacation-day concurrence, redeye restrictions, reserve-duty footprint, and positive-contact junior assignment.
  • The practical difference is not simply “better versus worse.” It is whether the contract mainly compensates the crewmember after disruption, or whether it narrows management discretion before the disruption tool is used.

Retirement Provisions

  • United locks in more actual retirement economics, including direct contributions, matching contributions, vesting, and legacy pension continuation for covered populations.
  • JetBlue protects participation in the company 401(k) plan and profit sharing if offered, but the posted agreement does not provide the same contract-level formula detail.
  • Retirement therefore matters here not only as a long-service issue, but as a contract-structure issue: formula certainty is much harder to harmonize than a single hourly-rate comparison.

CrewSignal Read

  • If Blue Sky remains a commercial partnership, this page is primarily a structural comparison exercise.
  • If the relationship ever deepened, the hardest issues to reconcile would likely be junior economics, reserve life, disruption control, insurance certainty, and retirement formulas.
  • The hardest sell to crews would probably not be the top-step rate. It would be any proposal that asks one side to accept weaker early-career pay, less predictable reserve life, or broader management latitude when operations break down.
