United vs JetBlue

Contract comparison — pay crediting, premium triggers, reserve architecture, and likely JCBA pressure points

Editor’s Note: This page compares the contract structures contained in the source documents listed below. It does not assume a merger. Blue Sky is a commercial partnership; this analysis exists to help crews and observers understand “alignment friction” if the relationship ever escalates into a consolidation pathway. Informational only; not legal advice.

Source documents reviewed

  • United: United Airlines / CWA-AFA Flight Attendants CBA (2016–2021) — pay schedules include base and incentive pay rate tables.
  • JetBlue: JetBlue / TWU Inflight Agreement document (pay tables include straight and premium pay scales through 1/1/2026; reserve and scheduling articles included).

Executive summary: the biggest alignment issues

  • Representation mismatch: United flight attendants are represented by CWA-AFA; JetBlue flight attendants are represented by TWU. In any single-carrier scenario, NMB representation mechanics (craft/class) would be unavoidable.
  • Premium pay triggers differ: United uses an “incentive pay” construct tied to quarterly credited time thresholds, while JetBlue uses a bid-period premium model (with a distinct premium pay scale and a defined threshold for premium compensation).
  • Reserve architecture is structurally different: both carriers use reserve concepts, but JetBlue’s reserve line construction rules and assignment mechanics are explicitly codified in ways that may not map cleanly to United’s system without negotiated translation.
  • International complexity vs network profile: United’s long-haul/international flying footprint creates work-rule requirements (pairing construction, rest, premium positions) that do not perfectly parallel JetBlue’s historic network structure. Any combined agreement must decide which rule set becomes the “pattern.”

Key provisions: side-by-side comparison

Topic United (CWA-AFA) JetBlue (TWU) Why it matters in an alignment scenario
Top-of-scale reference (examples) Base pay table shows top-step base rate reaching the high-$60/hr range in the referenced schedule; incentive rates are higher for credited time above the quarterly threshold. Straight scale reaches $62.25 at Year 13 (1/1/2026); Premium scale reaches $66.00 at Year 13 (1/1/2026). If a JCBA ever becomes necessary, the “pattern” question (which pay architecture wins) becomes immediate: hourly tables alone are not the whole story — the premium triggers matter.
Premium / incentive trigger “Incentive Pay Rates” apply to block hours credited above a quarterly threshold (e.g., credited time above 200 hours per quarter in the referenced schedule). Premium pay is tied to bid-period credit: premium scale applies to credited time above a defined threshold, and the agreement describes premium pay as 125% for credit above the threshold (with an election process for premium vs straight pay). Two different premium philosophies: “quarterly incentive” vs “bid-period premium.” This is a classic JCBA friction point.
Duty rig United includes a duty-rig concept summarized as 1 hour credit for each 2 hours on duty (plus pairing minimums such as a minimum one-day pairing value). JetBlue includes a duty-rig concept summarized as 1 hour credit for each 2 hours on duty, and also codifies minimum duty-period credit rules. Similar headline “1:2” duty-rig language can still hide major differences in pairing minimums, definitions, exclusions, and how credit is computed.
Reserve construction (example signal) Reserve systems exist, but are embedded in United’s broader scheduling architecture and may not mirror JetBlue’s line construction rules directly. JetBlue explicitly constructs reserve lines as blocks (e.g., 3–6 days) with defined days off and assignment rules including next-day assignment timing language. Even if pay tables align, reserve life can become the ratification “dealbreaker.” Merging reserve systems is often harder than merging wages.
Network-driven work rules United’s contract architecture supports a global network and associated pairing complexity. JetBlue’s agreement is built around JetBlue’s operating profile and staffing model. A combined agreement must decide whether international pairing rules are “upgraded,” “localized,” or handled with separate letter/appendix language.

Likely contentious JCBA pressure points

  • Premium pay philosophy: quarterly incentive vs bid-period premium (and how “above guarantee” is defined).
  • Reserve life + predictability: line construction, assignment timing, and days-off protections.
  • Pairing construction + crediting: duty rigs, minimum daily values, and which definitions control.
  • Representation mechanics: CWA-AFA vs TWU creates an additional layer: even “perfect” work-rule alignment doesn’t resolve the representation question.
