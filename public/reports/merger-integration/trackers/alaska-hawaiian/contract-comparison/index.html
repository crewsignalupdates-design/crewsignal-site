Alaska / Hawaiian — Contract Comparison

Side-by-side structure review of the Alaska and Hawaiian CWA-AFA agreements, with emphasis on the 2025 Hawaiian Comprehensive Extension Agreement and the most likely JCBA friction points.

Sources reviewed: Alaska CWA-AFA CBA (2025–2028) · Hawaiian CWA-AFA CBA (2020–2025) · Hawaiian Comprehensive Extension Agreement (2025)

Editor’s Note: This comparison is informational and focuses on observable contract structure. It is not legal advice, bargaining guidance, or a prediction of JCBA outcomes. Where contract language is complex or highly context-dependent, CrewSignal prioritizes the practical crew impact and the integration/JBCA implications rather than exhaustive clause-by-clause quotation.

Key Takeaways

  • The core structural gap is pay architecture: Alaska is built around a TFP-based system with distinct constructs (including boarding pay expressed in TFP), while Hawaiian is built around hourly flight-time pay plus premium pay and ground-time provisions.
  • The 2025 Hawaiian Comprehensive Extension Agreement is a bridge in several important ways: it aligns amendability timing with Alaska’s agreement, imports Alaska Air Group-style performance programs, and adjusts reserve/availability mechanics—without fully harmonizing the underlying pay system.
  • JCBA contention risk is highest where harmonization forces one group to “convert” into the other group’s structure: pay basis (TFP vs hourly), boarding/ground-time compensation, per diem/expense allowance standards, and reserve rules.

Documents in Scope

  • Alaska CWA-AFA CBA (2025–2028) — amendable Feb 28, 2028.
  • Hawaiian CWA-AFA CBA (2020–2025) — base agreement terms prior to extension.
  • Hawaiian Comprehensive Extension Agreement (2025) — extends/adjusts key terms and aligns amendability to Feb 28, 2028.

Pay Architecture

Why pay basis is the biggest JCBA problem

Alaska’s agreement expresses pay in a TFP-based architecture (including boarding pay expressed in TFP). Hawaiian’s agreement expresses pay in hourly flight-time pay with additional premium positions and defined ground-time pay triggers. A JCBA must ultimately choose one set of “units” as the dominant architecture or create a conversion system that crews view as fair.

Alaska: step rates + boarding pay expressed in TFP

  • Alaska’s base rates are expressed per TFP (with block-hour equivalents referenced in the contract).
  • Alaska boarding pay is explicitly stated as 0.50 TFP per segment (non-creditable to other pay calculations).

Hawaiian: hourly pay + premium pay + ground holding pay rules

  • Hawaiian hourly rates are stated by year of service (e.g., the agreement includes a Year 20+ step and a longevity add-on for 25+ years).
  • Hawaiian includes defined premium pay categories (e.g., galley/lead/“#1,” language, purser positions depending on equipment).
  • Hawaiian also includes ground holding pay triggers tied to elapsed ground time thresholds (originating vs through flights; domestic vs international).

JCBA Watch

If Alaska/Hawaiian JCBA talks move toward harmonizing “paid time,” the likely flashpoints are: (1) whether boarding pay exists as a per-segment entitlement, (2) what replaces Hawaiian ground-time triggers, and (3) whether any conversion produces “hidden” winners/losers across different trip mixes.

Expense Allowance / Per Diem

Both agreements provide time-based expense pay, but with different rate structures and trip categorization logic. In a JCBA, per diem becomes contentious when crews perceive that the harmonized structure undervalues the realities of their flying.

Alaska

  • Alaska per diem is stated as a single hourly rate with scheduled increases across the agreement term.
  • Start/stop timing is tied to required check-in and a defined post-arrival window.

Hawaiian

  • Hawaiian uses an hourly expense allowance with different rates (e.g., interisland vs international categories).
  • Start/stop timing is tied to a defined window before report and after return to domicile.

JCBA risk: Medium–High. Even small per diem changes have large cumulative effects and are easy for crews to benchmark against peers.

Performance Programs (Profit Sharing vs Performance-Based Pay)

One of the clearest “bridge” moves in the Hawaiian extension is the shift toward Alaska Air Group-style performance programs. This is an integration signal: it reduces structural separation in how company-wide performance value is distributed.

  • The extension replaces Hawaiian’s legacy profit-sharing framework with the Alaska Air Group performance-based pay plan.
  • The extension includes participation in Alaska’s Operational Performance Rewards (OPR) program.

JCBA risk: Medium. This is less “identity-level” than pay basis, but can become contentious if eligibility, metrics, or payout timing differ by group.

Reserve & Scheduling Mechanics

Structural note

Reserve systems are often where crews feel the integration pain first. Even when pay and benefits are improving, reserve rules control predictability, rest, and the lived experience of the job.

Extension “bridge” signals

  • The extension modifies reserve pick-up on guaranteed days off by tying eligibility to actual rest rather than scheduled rest.
  • The extension introduces an incentive concept: reserves who exceed a defined threshold on days off are paid at a 1.5x multiplier for hours above the threshold.
  • The extension adds open-flying visibility signals (operational transparency that affects how quickly open-time is absorbed and who gets access).

JCBA risk: Medium–High. Reserve harmonization is often a high-emotion issue because it impacts fatigue, family life, and scheduling control.

Most Likely JCBA Contention Points

  • Pay basis conversion: whether the JCBA uses TFP, hourly, or a hybrid—and whether conversion creates “silent” losers.
  • Boarding vs ground-time compensation: Alaska’s per-segment boarding pay vs Hawaiian ground holding pay constructs.
  • Expense/per diem harmonization: single-rate vs trip-category rates; start/stop timing; annual increases.
  • Reserve predictability & incentives: how availability windows, call-out, day-off pick-up, and premium multipliers are unified.
  • Premium pay & positions: how lead/galley/purser/language premiums are aligned across fleets and service models.
  • Performance pay programs: metric definitions, payout cadence, and eligibility consistency across the combined group.

What to Watch in Weekly Integration Updates

  • Conversion language: any public wording suggesting “harmonizing pay basis” or “standardizing duty credit.”
  • Reserve system pilots: trial changes, cross-utilization concepts, or standardized availability windows.
  • Performance pay implementation: first real payout cycle under the new extension framework and how it is communicated.
  • Expense alignment: any “interim” per diem changes (interim is often where disputes start).
