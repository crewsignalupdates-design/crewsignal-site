  • Systems & customer-facing integration: Alaska has described the spring 2026 move to a single passenger service system as a key step toward a single booking platform, including changes to how flights are presented and how codes are applied.

  • External pressure signals: Continued public reporting on litigation and route/competition claims is a reminder that “integration mechanics” can become part of the public and regulatory narrative even after deal close.

CrewSignal Assessment

The most meaningful “integration indicator” this week is the continued sequencing: the company’s public integration roadmap is still organized around a major systems milestone in spring 2026 (reservation-system consolidation), while the labor-side integration work is proceeding section-by-section through the joint negotiations process.

The JNC update suggests incremental forward movement on work-rule architecture (leave, expenses, and domiciles), while higher-friction topics (seniority integration mechanics, grievance procedures, and charter structures) remain queued for later discussion. For crews, that usually implies a longer runway before full “single-platform” clarity exists across the merged operation.

  • Watch for any training, comms, or policy updates that align with the spring 2026 passenger service system cutover timeline.
  • Watch for whether and when the JNC begins formal movement on the “on hold” sections (seniority, grievance/enforcement, charters).
  • Continue to monitor whether litigation or external scrutiny changes integration pacing, public messaging, or roadmap commitments.
