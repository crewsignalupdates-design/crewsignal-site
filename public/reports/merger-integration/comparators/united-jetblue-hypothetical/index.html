Shared methodology and assumptions

This package treats the two transactions as hypothetical Flight Attendant integration exercises, not as established merger transactions. The comparator analysis uses AirTran/Southwest for negotiated cross-union seniority integration and Continental/United for single-transportation-system and representation-process sequencing. [S32] [S33] [S12] [S13]

The reports distinguish four layers that often get conflated: corporate transaction approval, NMB representation, McCaskill-Bond/Allegheny-Mohawk seniority integration, and joint-contract bargaining. A merger announcement, if one ever occurs, would not itself decide the Flight Attendant representative, seniority list, base rights, furlough protections, or contract terms. [S8]

The economic sections are intentionally preliminary. United TA2 is treated as a conditional benchmark pending ratification status, while American/APFA 2024 is treated as a ratified CBA baseline. JetBlue/TWU public contract text is treated as a working source pending verification against any member-only official copy and active LOAs. [S20] [S21] [S25] [S26]

Legal and representation-process concepts

2.1 Single transportation system

In a merger context, the NMB first examines whether the carriers have become a single transportation system for the relevant craft or class. The NMB representation manual lists practical indicia such as combined operations, common marketing or aircraft markings, standardized uniforms, integrated personnel functions, common management, and a combined workforce. [S8]

The Continental/United comparator illustrates the sequence: CWA-AFA asked the NMB to determine whether United, Continental, and Continental Micronesia were a single transportation system for Flight Attendants; the Board found a single system, then moved to representation questions. [S12]

2.2 Showing of interest

The NMB showing-of-interest requirement is the threshold that allows an applicant to proceed in a representation matter. In merger cases, after a single-transportation-system finding, applicants must submit evidence of representation or a showing of interest from at least 50% of the employees in the combined craft or class. [S8]

This requirement is critical for United/JetBlue. JetBlue-only support would not necessarily satisfy the threshold for a combined United/JetBlue Flight Attendant craft or class, especially if the United Flight Attendant group is substantially larger. Any TWU, CWA-AFA, or other application would be judged against the combined unit at the relevant NMB point in time. [S8] [S24] [S9]

2.3 Majority absorption / certification extension

This draft uses majority absorption as shorthand for NMB certification extension in a merger setting. It means a representation outcome in which the NMB extends an incumbent representative to the combined craft or class, usually where the employee groups are not comparable in size, no intervenor makes the required showing, or the other incumbent does not seek certification. It does not mean seniority stapling, automatic contract absorption, or loss of base protections. [S10] [S11]

The American/US Airways Flight Attendant case is the cleanest example for this report. APFA represented American Flight Attendants; CWA-AFA represented US Airways Flight Attendants. After a single-system process, CWA-AFA did not seek certification for the combined group, no intervenor filed within the NMB period, and the NMB extended APFA certification to the new American Flight Attendant craft or class. [S10]

The Alaska/Virgin America case is another useful Flight Attendant example: the NMB extended CWA-AFA certification to the combined Alaska/Virgin America Flight Attendant craft or class. This supports the concept that the NMB can extend one incumbent certification rather than hold a contested election, but only through the NMB process. [S11]

2.4 Representation is separate from seniority integration and the JCBA

The representation result identifies the certified representative for the craft or class. It does not determine where any Flight Attendant sits on the integrated seniority list, whether any base is protected, whether furloughs are barred, or which CBA terms become the post-merger joint agreement. Those issues must be handled through seniority-integration procedures, scope/successorship protections, labor-protective provisions, and bargaining. [S8] [S14] [S15] [S22] [S26]

Scope-protection and successorship summary

Scope, successorship, and merger clauses are the contract architecture that protects Flight Attendant work while representation and seniority questions are being resolved. The contract model should combine the strongest parts of each source rather than average the contracts.

The model contract should combine all three where relevant. [S15] [S22] [S23] [S26]

Report A: United/JetBlue hypothetical comparator

5.1 Executive thesis

United/JetBlue is best modeled as AirTran/Southwest for negotiated cross-union seniority integration, supplemented by Continental/United for NMB single-system and representation mechanics. The dominant representational process question is whether CWA-AFA could obtain certification extension/majority absorption after a single-transportation-system finding, or whether TWU could make the necessary combined-system showing of interest to force a contested representation case. [S8] [S10] [S11] [S12] [S13] [S32] [S33]

5.3 Representation pathways: showing of interest and majority absorption

A United/JetBlue merger would begin, for representation purposes, with the NMB single-transportation-system inquiry. Existing certifications would not disappear merely because a transaction was announced; the NMB process would control any change in certified representation. [S8] [S12]

CWA-AFA would likely have a strong certification-extension or majority-absorption argument if the combined Flight Attendant craft or class were numerically dominated by the United side and if no other applicant made the required showing. The analogy is strongest to Alaska/Virgin America and American/US Airways, where the NMB extended an incumbent certification in the combined Flight Attendant craft or class. [S10] [S11] [S24]

TWU would remain the certified representative at JetBlue unless and until the NMB altered that status. To contest representation on the combined system, TWU would likely need a valid showing of interest from at least 50% of the combined United/JetBlue Flight Attendant craft or class, not merely support from JetBlue inflight crewmembers. This makes the showing-of-interest threshold a central practical issue for TWU. [S8] [S9]

It should not be assumed that CWA-AFA would absorb JetBlue. The precise formulation is that CWA-AFA would have a plausible certification-extension argument after a single-system finding; that representation outcome would not decide seniority integration, base protection, no-furlough protection, or the post-merger contract. [S8] [S14] [S22] [S26]

5.4 TWU dedicated-local model versus CWA-AFA multi-carrier model

TWU is best described as a broader transport union that uses dedicated airline-specific Flight Attendant locals. TWU Local 579 identifies itself as the union of JetBlue Flight Attendants, while TWU Local 556 identifies itself as the union of Southwest Flight Attendants. TWU also publicly identifies Flight Attendant locals at Southwest, Allegiant, and JetBlue. [S28] [S29] [S30]

CWA-AFA should be described as a multi-carrier Flight Attendant union with an active organizing model. Its public materials state that it is made up of Flight Attendants from 20 airlines and include active organizing pages for additional carriers. Whether that model stretches resources or dilutes attention from a particular airline is an evaluative argument, not an established fact. [S31]

If a United/JetBlue transaction proceeded, a temporary Flight Attendant merger council, that preserves CWA-AFA United local/domicile structures and TWU Local 579 JetBlue legacy participation, would be created until the NMB representation issue, seniority integration, and post-merger base elections are resolved. [S14] [S26] [S28]

5.5 Seniority integration architecture

AirTran/Southwest is the strongest comparator for cross-union Flight Attendant seniority integration. Southwest announced that Southwest/TWU Local 556 and AirTran/CWA-AFA Council 57 reached a tentative seniority integration agreement, which was later ratified by both Flight Attendant groups. That model supports direct union-to-union negotiation, membership validation where feasible, and arbitration fallback if negotiation fails. [S32] [S33]

JetBlue/TWU Article 3 already contains an integration framework based on McCaskill-Bond and Allegheny-Mohawk principles, with explicit no-bumping and no-system-rebid protections. CWA-AFA Section X provides a formal union-policy framework for merger representatives, seniority-integration process, transition governance, and dispute resolution. [S14] [S26]

  • No stapling and no automatic contract absorption.
  • Separate operations and no FA interchange until seniority integration and post-merger rates/rules/working conditions are complete. [S22] [S26]
  • Base protection should preserve JetBlue anti-bumping concepts for all legacy bases and avoid system-wide realignment solely because of seniority-list integration. [S26]
  • Expedited arbitration should apply to scope, successorship, seniority-integration timing, no-interchange, no-furlough, and base-protection disputes. [S22] [S26]

5.6 Scope and contract architecture priorities

A United/JetBlue model contract should combine United/CWA-AFA TA2 Section 1 and LOA 16 with JetBlue/TWU Article 3. The combined scope clause should preserve United anti-alter-ego protections, the United Express carve-out as a defined exception, JetBlue exclusive flying language, JetBlue base anti-bumping language, and no-interchange language. [S22] [S23] [S26]

Economic architecture should use a higher-of formula rather than selecting one contract wholesale. Because United TA2 was pending through its 2026 ratification timeline, the draft should say "if ratified" or "as a bargaining target" when using TA2 economics. JetBlue-specific ground-duty, airport-standby, LOD, holiday, and base protections should be preserved as distinct contract items rather than erased by legacy-carrier harmonization. [S21] [S25] [S26]

5.7 DOJ-risk note: supported speculation only

United/JetBlue should be described as a New York/Boston and constrained-airport competition case rather than a predictable approval case. DOJ precedent from the American/JetBlue Northeast Alliance and JetBlue/Spirit matters because both involved JetBlue's role as an independent competitor and low-fare competitive force. [S36] [S37] [S38]

If DOJ entertained remedies rather than suing to block a hypothetical United/JetBlue transaction, the supported speculation is that remedies would likely focus on constrained airport assets, especially slots, gates, and facilities, and on transition-period coordination limits. That inference is drawn from airline-merger precedent such as the American/US Airways settlement, not from any known United/JetBlue remedy proposal. [S34] [S35]

Report caution: there is no established United/JetBlue transaction and no public DOJ remedy package for one, nor should one be inferred.[S1] [S2] [S3] [S4]

Shared best-of model contract architecture

The model contract should be built as a highest-protection, highest-economic-value matrix. The guiding rule is not "pick the surviving airline's contract," but "use the strongest Flight Attendant protection available from the source contracts and comparator models, while preserving transition fairness."
