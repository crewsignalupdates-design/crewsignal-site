Shared methodology and assumptions

This package treats the two transactions as hypothetical Flight Attendant integration exercises, not as established merger transactions. The comparator analysis uses AirTran/Southwest for negotiated cross-union seniority integration and Continental/United for single-transportation-system and representation-process sequencing. [S32] [S33] [S12] [S13]

The reports distinguish four layers that often get conflated: corporate transaction approval, NMB representation, McCaskill-Bond/Allegheny-Mohawk seniority integration, and joint-contract bargaining. A merger announcement, if one ever occurs, would not itself decide the Flight Attendant representative, seniority list, base rights, furlough protections, or contract terms. [S8]

The economic sections are intentionally preliminary. United TA2 is treated as a conditional benchmark pending ratification status, while American/APFA 2024 is treated as a ratified CBA baseline. JetBlue/TWU public contract text is treated as a working source pending verification against any member-only official copy and active LOAs. [S20] [S21] [S25] [S26]

Legal and representation-process concepts

2.1 Single transportation system

In a merger context, the NMB first examines whether the carriers have become a single transportation system for the relevant craft or class. The NMB representation manual lists practical indicia such as combined operations, common marketing or aircraft markings, standardized uniforms, integrated personnel functions, common management, and a combined workforce. [S8]

The Continental/United comparator illustrates the sequence: CWA-AFA asked the NMB to determine whether United, Continental, and Continental Micronesia were a single transportation system for Flight Attendants; the Board found a single system, then moved to representation questions. [S12]

2.2 Showing of interest

The NMB showing-of-interest requirement is the threshold that allows an applicant to proceed in a representation matter. In merger cases, after a single-transportation-system finding, applicants must submit evidence of representation or a showing of interest from at least 50% of the employees in the combined craft or class. [S8]

This requirement is critical for United/JetBlue. JetBlue-only support would not necessarily satisfy the threshold for a combined United/JetBlue Flight Attendant craft or class, especially if the United Flight Attendant group is substantially larger. Any TWU, CWA-AFA, or other application would be judged against the combined unit at the relevant NMB point in time. [S8] [S24] [S9]

2.3 Majority absorption / certification extension

This draft uses majority absorption as shorthand for NMB certification extension in a merger setting. It means a representation outcome in which the NMB extends an incumbent representative to the combined craft or class, usually where the employee groups are not comparable in size, no intervenor makes the required showing, or the other incumbent does not seek certification. It does not mean seniority stapling, automatic contract absorption, or loss of base protections. [S10] [S11]

The American/US Airways Flight Attendant case is the cleanest example for this report. APFA represented American Flight Attendants; CWA-AFA represented US Airways Flight Attendants. After a single-system process, CWA-AFA did not seek certification for the combined group, no intervenor filed within the NMB period, and the NMB extended APFA certification to the new American Flight Attendant craft or class. [S10]

The Alaska/Virgin America case is another useful Flight Attendant example: the NMB extended CWA-AFA certification to the combined Alaska/Virgin America Flight Attendant craft or class. This supports the concept that the NMB can extend one incumbent certification rather than hold a contested election, but only through the NMB process. [S11]

2.4 Representation is separate from seniority integration and the JCBA

The representation result identifies the certified representative for the craft or class. It does not determine where any Flight Attendant sits on the integrated seniority list, whether any base is protected, whether furloughs are barred, or which CBA terms become the post-merger joint agreement. Those issues must be handled through seniority-integration procedures, scope/successorship protections, labor-protective provisions, and bargaining. [S8] [S14] [S15] [S22] [S26]

Scope-protection and successorship summary

Scope, successorship, and merger clauses are the contract architecture that protects Flight Attendant work while representation and seniority questions are being resolved. The contract model should combine the strongest parts of each source rather than average the contracts.

The model contract should combine all three where relevant. [S15] [S22] [S23] [S26]

Report B: American/United hypothetical comparator

6.1 Executive thesis

American/United is structurally closer to Continental/United than AirTran/Southwest because it would involve two large legacy Flight Attendant groups, two mature incumbent representatives, and likely a contested NMB representation process after any single-transportation-system finding. AirTran/Southwest remains useful for seniority-integration process design, but not as the dominant representation model. [S12] [S13] [S32] [S33]

6.2 Established facts and assumptions

6.3 Representation model: APFA one-airline-only versus CWA-AFA multi-carrier

American/United should not be drafted as a simple majority-absorption case. Unlike United/JetBlue, it would likely involve two large legacy Flight Attendant groups and two strong incumbent representatives. Unless one union agreed not to contest representation, the more realistic first-pass assumption is a contested NMB process after any single-system finding. [S8] [S10] [S12] [S13]

APFA's strongest governance claim is the one-airline-only, Flight Attendant-only model. APFA public materials identify it as the certified representative for American Airlines Flight Attendants, with national officers, base presidents, base vice presidents, and base-level representation within the union's structure. [S18] [S19]

The draft should also be precise about the limit of that label. APFA is one-airline-only at American today. If APFA represented a merged American/United craft or class, the model would become one independent Flight Attendant-only union for the combined airline, not an American-only union. [S18] [S19]

CWA-AFA's strongest representational claim would be its existing United incumbency, public merger-policy infrastructure, multi-carrier Flight Attendant experience, and local/domicile representation model. Its multi-carrier and organizing footprint are established by public materials, but resource dilution should remain an evaluative argument unless separately supported by evidence. [S14] [S21] [S22] [S31]

6.4 Continental/United comparator lessons

Continental/United shows that corporate merger and representation resolution can precede contract integration by years. The NMB found a United/Continental single transportation system, then certified CWA-AFA after a Flight Attendant representation election in which CWA-AFA received 11,942 votes and IAM received 9,745 votes. [S12] [S13]

The comparator lesson for American/United is timing discipline: the report should not imply that corporate closing produces instant contract merger, instant base integration, or instant list integration. A post-merger joint Flight Attendant agreement would likely be the central labor challenge. [S12] [S13]

6.5 Seniority integration architecture

American/United seniority integration should be modeled as negotiated legacy-to-legacy integration with binding arbitration fallback under McCaskill-Bond/Allegheny-Mohawk principles. The American/US Airways CWA-AFA/APFA seniority protocol is useful, but it should not be treated as automatically controlling because American/United would involve different group sizes, unions, and bargaining leverage. [S17] [S14] [S15]

  • No stapling and no contract absorption by default.
  • No system flush, system-wide rebid, or base displacement solely because of the merged seniority list. [S17] [S26]
  • Separate bidding, reserve, and pairing systems should continue until the merged seniority list and JCBA are operationally ready. [S22] [S26]
  • Seniority integration should not waive LPPs, partial-transaction protections, or no-furlough provisions. [S15] [S22]

6.6 Scope and contract architecture priorities

American/United should be drafted as a true best-of legacy JCBA, not as one side absorbing the other. The strongest package would combine American/APFA Section 1 LPP and partial-transaction protections with United/CWA-AFA TA2 no-interchange, no-premature-integration, no-merger-caused-furlough, and LOA 16 scope language. [S15] [S22] [S23]

The economic model should use a higher-of matrix for hourly rates, premiums, boarding, sit/holding time, international and language premiums, lead/purser/galley duties, reserve, reassignments, schedule protections, hotels, safety, and ground-duty pay. The model should not average the contracts or treat a representation outcome as a contract outcome. [S16] [S21] [S22] [S25]

6.7 DOJ-risk note: supported speculation only

American/United has stronger antitrust-risk support in independent reporting than United/JetBlue. Reuters reported that a United/American deal would create a carrier with roughly 40% of U.S. domestic capacity based on 2025 schedules and cited expert skepticism that divestitures could fix the competition problem. [S7]

If DOJ entertained remedies rather than challenging the transaction, the only supported remedy category to discuss at this stage is structural divestiture of slots, gates, and facilities at constrained or overlap-heavy airports. That inference is based on DOJ's American/US Airways settlement and final judgment materials, not on any known American/United remedy package. [S34] [S35]

Suggested report posture: American/United would likely face extraordinary DOJ scrutiny. Approval should not be assumed; indeed, a DOJ challenge may be a more plausible first-pass assumption than a predictable settlement. [S7] [S34] [S35]

Shared best-of model contract architecture

The model contract should be built as a highest-protection, highest-economic-value matrix. The guiding rule is not "pick the surviving airline's contract," but "use the strongest Flight Attendant protection available from the source contracts and comparator models, while preserving transition fairness."
