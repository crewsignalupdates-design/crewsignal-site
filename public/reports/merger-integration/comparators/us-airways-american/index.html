Merger & Integration

US Airways / American

Contract Comparison

This report consolidates the prior contract matrix, representation explainer, source map, American bankruptcy research, and forward 2024-CBA bridge into a single main report structure. It is informational and comparative only. It does not replace governing contract text, side letters, past practice, arbitration awards, or official union/company interpretations.

Sources and method. This report compares what the combined US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement brought into the American merger against what legacy American / APFA brought through the 2001 agreement as modified by the 2003 Restructuring Participation Agreement, the Foundation Document, the bankruptcy-era LBFO / New CBA stack, and the Conditional Labor Agreement (CLA) bridge. It then traces the transition architecture created by the Agreement on Bargaining and Representation (ABR), Negotiations Protocol Agreement (NPA), Merger Transition Agreement (MTA), National Mediation Board certification, JCBA arbitration, and later 2024 CBA bridge. Post-2004 US Airways-side narrative uses CWA-AFA unless preserving a source title or quotation.

Core finding

The strongest historical comparison is not that APFA escaped bankruptcy pressure while CWA-AFA did not. Both flight-attendant groups arrived at the New American through bankruptcy-conditioned agreements. The cleaner finding is narrower: APFA reached the merger with a more legible bankruptcy-to-merger contract stack, while CWA-AFA reached the same merger with a newly combined but late-arriving LUS book that still carried the scars of the America West / US Airways integration.

Legacy American brought a traceable sequence: 2001 APFA/American agreement; 2003 Restructuring Participation Agreement and bankruptcy-protection layer; 2011–2012 Chapter 11 / Section 1113 pressure; LBFO / New CBA bridge; CLA; and then the ABR / NPA / MTA path to a joint agreement. LUS brought the February 28, 2013 combined US Airways / CWA-AFA Red Book — a real combined book, but one that arrived only after years of East/West non-parity, rejected tentative agreements, and public union pressure.

Handoff from the final LUS Red Book

The prior America West / US Airways report should end where this report begins: with LUS finally operating under a combined US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement. The Red Book unified the former East and West contract histories, superseded prior agreements except for specified continuing letters, and created the LUS source agreement that entered the American merger. It did not erase the underlying history: East’s pension termination, bankruptcy-era concessions, and the long East/West integration delay remained part of the institutional record.

Legacy American / APFA bankruptcy-to-merger stack

The legacy American baseline should be framed as a contract stack, not as a single static book. The Foundation Document was designed as a working representation of the 2001 APFA/American agreement as modified by the 2003 Restructuring Participation Agreement. The 2003 layer is the American counterpart to the US Airways restructuring layers: it lowered or deferred value in exchange for bankruptcy-avoidance and protection language, while preserving a clearer textual platform for later bargaining.

The Wayback recovery work materially improves this section. The recovered APFA source family includes the Section 1113 explanation, retirement outline, RPA pay-rate attachment, Appendix S attachment, medical attachment, equity / stock materials, profit-sharing materials, pension letter, and later JCBA award / implementation files. Until the recovered PDFs are text-extracted and checked, those files should be cited in the source map as recovered source leads; the substantive terms should continue to rely on the Foundation Document, bankruptcy Q&A, AMR filings, court filings, and the uploaded 2024 CBA legacy letters.

LUS / CWA-AFA baseline before the American merger

The LUS baseline was not the original America West agreement or the original US Airways East 2000 agreement. By the American merger context, the operative book was the 2013 combined US Airways / CWA-AFA Red Book. That distinction matters. LUS entered the merger with one combined source agreement, but the book itself was the product of a long and difficult integration process; in particular, the former US Airways flight-attendant defined-benefit pension plan had already been terminated and moved to PBGC trusteeship years before the American merger.

Representation and transition architecture

The representation outcome at the New American was not the product of a late public campaign victory by APFA over CWA-AFA. It was a negotiated, member-ratified handoff that was then processed through the National Mediation Board’s merger procedures. That distinction matters because it changes the historical question. The question is not why CWA-AFA lost an ordinary head-to-head election; the question is why CWA-AFA agreed to convert its US Airways representation into a managed transition after years in which the America West / US Airways integration had still not delivered prompt parity.

The Agreement on Bargaining and Representation (ABR) did more than identify the future representative. It created the core transition architecture. APFA would become the representative of the combined flight-attendant craft or class at the New American. CWA-AFA would notify the NMB that it would not seek certification for the post-merger craft or class. The opening bargaining baselines would be the US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement and the APFA agreement as amended by the Conditional Labor Agreement (CLA). Former US Airways flight attendants would receive interim representation inside the APFA structure, CWA-AFA would continue servicing the pre-merger US Airways agreement during the transition, and former US Airways flight attendants would not be charged an APFA initiation fee.

There was still an NMB representation process after the single-carrier step, but it was not a normal organizing campaign. Once the single-transportation-system case moved forward, CWA-AFA had already agreed not to seek certification for the merged craft or class. After the NMB found a single transportation system, potential intervenors had a short and demanding window: thirty days to file with a showing of interest from at least fifty percent of the combined craft or class. No intervenor filed. The NMB then treated the APFA and CWA-AFA pre-merger numbers as not comparable and extended APFA certification to the combined American Airlines flight-attendant craft or class.

This also means the older 35 percent figure should not be used for this case. That lower threshold belongs to an earlier NMB merger-procedure period. The American / US Airways flight-attendant case used the post-2012 merger rules: a thirty-day intervention period and a fifty-percent showing of interest. Likewise, any familiar one-year concept belongs to card dating or bar-rule discussions, not to the merger-specific intervention window that applied after the single-carrier finding here.

Six-month single-carrier commitment and 150-day bargaining clock

The report should separate two deadlines that are easy to conflate. The six-month commitment was a representation / NMB timing issue: AFA/APFA member-facing materials described a commitment to jointly file the single-carrier petition with the NMB within six months of the December 9, 2013 merger close. It was not the deadline for mediation.

The JCBA bargaining clock came from the NPA. Negotiations would begin within the defined post-approval window and then continue for no more than 150 additional days unless the parties agreed otherwise. If the parties did not reach a tentative agreement, or if a tentative agreement was rejected, the unresolved issues would proceed through mediation and then to final and binding interest arbitration. That backstop became decisive after the 2014 tentative agreement was rejected.

APFA / CWA-AFA maintenance and dues arrangement

The APFA-to-CWA-AFA payment language should not be described as a simple payoff for CWA-AFA standing down. The better reading is contractual and administrative. After APFA certification, CWA-AFA continued to provide transition services for former US Airways flight attendants under the US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement. APFA reimbursed CWA-AFA from dues collected from those former US Airways flight attendants because CWA-AFA was still administering and enforcing the legacy agreement and still handling legacy grievances.

The arrangement had two practical stages. First, from APFA certification until the JCBA became effective plus ninety days — extendable in ninety-day increments for up to one year — CWA-AFA continued administering and enforcing the US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement, and APFA paid CWA-AFA 100 percent of the dues paid to APFA by pre-merger US Airways flight attendants. Second, CWA-AFA continued handling pending grievances under the US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement until those matters concluded, but no later than two years after the JCBA became effective, with reimbursement declining over time.

That does not eliminate criticism. It clarifies the criticism. CWA-AFA preserved an enforcement tail, bargaining baseline, interim representation path, and transition resources. But it did so only after years in which the East/West integration had still not produced timely parity. The maintenance agreement therefore cuts both ways: it shows that the handoff was not an abandonment of LUS members, but it also shows how late the handoff occurred and how much leverage had already shifted toward a managed exit.

2014 TA, rejection, arbitration, and JCBA implementation

The final JCBA should be framed as the result of a negotiated process plus member action, not as a simple management imposition. The NPA contemplated ratification. When the 2014 tentative agreement was rejected, the pre-agreed mediation / interest-arbitration backstop controlled. The award and implementation letter then produced the first American / APFA JCBA for the combined flight-attendant craft or class.

This sequence is important for evaluation. Union leaders negotiated the architecture; members retained a ratification vote; member rejection triggered the arbitration path; management benefited from the fixed backstop; and the RLA / NMB representation machinery structured the transition independently of contract merits.

Objective evaluation of union handling

The report should be candid without overstating causation. CWA-AFA’s record on US Airways was not passive: it preserved a combined LUS book, secured a role in New American bargaining, maintained contract administration through the handoff, and preserved grievance servicing. But it also entered the American merger late, after a prolonged failure to deliver East/West parity and after the US Airways flight-attendant DB pension loss had already become irreversible.

APFA’s record was not concession-free. APFA accepted a 2003 restructuring layer and later navigated AMR Chapter 11 and the LBFO / New CBA path. The meaningful contrast is that APFA reached the merger with a more coherent bankruptcy-to-merger bridge and a frozen, not PBGC-terminated, legacy flight-attendant DB plan. The report should therefore compare outcomes while testing the reasons: timing, court posture, PBGC leverage, plan funding, management strategy, union structure, member ratification, and merger leverage.

Bridge to the 2024 APFA / American CBA

The uploaded American / APFA CBA is not a separate 2020–2024 successor agreement. The cover identifies it as a collective bargaining agreement effective September 12, 2024 through September 11, 2029. Its table of contents preserves legacy letters for Appendix S and Article 36 of the 2001/2003 CBA, the LAA Article 36 Retirement Benefit Plan, and the LUS Defined Benefit Pension Plan. That makes the 2024 CBA the correct next-contract comparator after the 2014 JCBA / 2019 amendable period.
