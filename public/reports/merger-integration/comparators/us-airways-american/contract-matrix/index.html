US Airways / American

Contract Comparison Matrix

Method note. This is a historical matrix, not a JCBA redline. It compares the February 28, 2013 combined US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement with the APFA American-side baseline represented by the Foundation Document and the CLA bridge.
Topic US Airways / LUS baseline American / LAA baseline Historical implication
Book carried into New American bargaining 2013 combined US Airways / CWA-AFA agreement APFA agreement as amended by the CLA / Foundation Document The ABR explicitly treats these as the two opening baselines.
How the baseline was assembled Late-arriving combined book after prolonged East/West delay and parity disputes. Compiled 2001 agreement as modified in 2003, then bridged during the merger. American’s side is easier to narrate and easier to use as a bargaining platform.
Recognition and merger protection One combined agreement recognizes both the legacy US Airways and America West certifications. Foundation Document Article 1 includes recognition, successorship, and fair-and-equitable merger / acquisition protection. Both sides had protection language, but the APFA side was already compiled in one working text.
Bankruptcy conditioning LUS inherited the legacy burden of US Airways restructuring and the already-terminated flight-attendant pension plan. LAA inherited the 2003 restructuring deal, 2011–2012 Chapter 11, and a frozen rather than terminated flight-attendant pension plan. Both were bankruptcy-shaped, but the American-side bridge was more legible by merger time.
Transition design The LUS book existed, but immediately became one input into the New American stack. CLA, ABR, NPA, and MTA openly framed the move from bridge deal to joint contract. APFA’s transition mechanics were more explicit and more systematized on paper.
Representation path CWA-AFA continued representing former US Airways flight attendants until APFA certification and then shifted into a maintenance-agreement role. APFA became the certified representative of the combined craft or class at the New American. The representation issue was negotiated before it became an election fight.
Retirement / benefits posture By merger time, the flight-attendant defined-benefit pension loss was already historical fact. The flight-attendant defined-benefit plan was frozen effective November 1, 2012, with replacement defined-contribution benefits for eligible non-pilot employees. The retirement baseline carried into the merger was materially different.
Bottom-line read CWA-AFA finally arrived at a combined book, but late. APFA brought a cleaner bankruptcy-to-merger bridge into the New American process. That is the clearest historical contrast supported by the record.
